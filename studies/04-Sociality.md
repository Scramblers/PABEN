# Social Behavior as Social X-Fields

## A PABEN Study of Social Emotion, Attractors, Aversion and Emergent Regulation

**Author:** Niels Christoffer Poulsen  
**Status:** Speculative study / working hypothesis  
**Date:** July 2026  

---

## Abstract

This study proposes a PABEN interpretation of social behavior as movement within social X-fields. The central claim is that social structures do not primarily regulate individuals from above. Rather, social structures emerge from many individual agents repeatedly evaluating persons, relations, groups, institutions and sacred orders as social contexts that either increase or decrease their possibility of continuation.

This claim is compatible with several lines of social psychology and behavioral science: humans show a strong need to belong, social proximity can regulate emotion and reduce effort, and social exclusion or ostracism has measurable affective and cognitive consequences [Baumeister & Leary, 1995; Beckes & Coan, 2011; Eisenberger et al., 2003; Williams, 2007]. At the same time, the claim conflicts with or must be limited by sociological traditions that treat social facts, institutions and collective meanings as external and coercive realities that cannot be reduced to individual experience alone [Durkheim, 1895; Searle, 1995].

In this reading, a person, group, institution or religious order can function as a social context. Each agent evaluates such contexts through X: Trustability, Executability, Reachability and Valencability. If a social context increases X, it becomes attractive. If it lowers X, it becomes aversive. Social emotions are the felt gradients of these social X-evaluations. Pride, shame, guilt, gratitude, longing, humiliation, forgiveness, indignation and belonging are not secondary decorations on social life. They are the emotional forces through which agents approach, avoid, preserve, repair or reject social contexts. This is broadly consistent with research treating shame, guilt, pride and gratitude as self-conscious or moral emotions with social-regulatory functions [Tangney et al., 2007; Tracy & Robins, 2007; Algoe, 2012].

Groups, norms, institutions and religions are therefore not treated as independent organisms above the individual. They are interpreted here as emergent regulatory fields produced by repeated individual attempts to increase X, especially through shared joy, recognition, belonging, relief, forgiveness and valid participation. This is a PABEN hypothesis. It should be read alongside alternative views in which groups and institutions have emergent properties, collective intentionality, or external coercive force that cannot be fully derived from individual evaluation [Durkheim, 1895; Searle, 1995; Ostrom, 1990].

## 1. Framing

PABEN describes agency through Perception / Contexts, Action / Skills, Beliefs, Experience and Needs. In its behavioral form, a Belief carries an action while a context remains usable:

```text
do Skill while Context
```

In social life, the context is often another agent or a shared social field. A parent, friend, group, school, workplace, court, nation, public audience or sacred order can all function as contexts that carry or block behavior. Attachment theory, belongingness research and social baseline theory all support the general idea that other people are not merely optional external stimuli, but part of the regulatory ecology in which human action and emotion normally operate [Ainsworth et al., 1978; Baumeister & Leary, 1995; Beckes & Coan, 2011].

The central question of social behavior is therefore:

> Which Beliefs become viable under this social context?

This shifts the study away from top-down systems and toward the repeated X-evaluations of individual agents. Social structures become stable only because many agents continue to approach, preserve, defend, repair or fear the same social contexts. This is close to psychological and game-theoretic accounts of norms as expectation-dependent, but it should not erase the sociological point that norms and institutions can confront individuals as already existing external structures [Bicchieri, 2006; Durkheim, 1895].

## 2. Social X

In PABEN, X evaluates the viability of continuation. For social behavior, X should be applied to Beliefs under social contexts:

```text
X_social(B | C_social) = R(B | C_social) * V(B | C_social) * Q(B | C_social) * T(B | C_social)
```

Where `C_social` can be a person, relation, group, institution, role, public scene, ritual, moral order or sacred context.

Strictly speaking, the agent does not evaluate the social context alone. The agent evaluates which Beliefs can be carried, opened, blocked, stabilized or threatened under that context. A person, group or institution becomes attractive when it makes valuable Beliefs more reachable, executable, trustworthy and need-relieving. It becomes aversive when it blocks, destabilizes or threatens the Beliefs through which the agent can continue.

This claim is supported indirectly by research showing that social proximity can reduce perceived effort and threat, while rejection, ostracism and isolation can increase distress and disrupt regulation [Beckes & Coan, 2011; Eisenberger et al., 2003; Williams, 2007]. It is also consistent with the need-to-belong hypothesis, which argues that stable, non-aversive interpersonal bonds are a fundamental human motivation [Baumeister & Leary, 1995].

The components can be read socially:

| X component | Social interpretation |
|---|---|
| Trustability / T | Can this social context carry my beliefs stably? |
| Executability / Q | Can I act here without excessive correction, danger, shame or instability? |
| Reachability / R | Does this context open realistic routes for continuation? |
| Valencability / V | Does this context provide access to joy, relief, recognition, belonging, care, meaning or need fulfillment? |

The social world is therefore not neutral. It is felt as a landscape of attractors, aversive fields and unstable fields. This is a PABEN formulation; it overlaps with but is not identical to existing concepts such as affordances, social identity, social norms, attachment, belongingness and social baseline regulation.

## 3. Attractors and Aversive Fields

A social context becomes an attractor when it reliably increases X. It becomes aversive when it lowers X or threatens future continuation.

| Social X-pattern | Field character | Typical behavior |
|---|---|---|
| High positive X | Attractor | Approach, preserve, imitate, defend |
| Low or negative X | Aversive field | Avoid, resist, escape, reject |
| Ambivalent X | Unstable field | Monitor, appease, hesitate, reflect |
| High V but low T/Q | Tempting but dangerous | Desire mixed with fear |
| High T but low V | Safe but empty | Compliance without joy |
| High R but low permission | Visible but forbidden route | Longing, frustration, secrecy |
| High group X, low self X | Conformity pressure | Alienation, suppression, shame-risk |
| High self X, low group X | Deviance pressure | Conflict, exposure, possible exclusion |

The key point is that social behavior follows felt X-gradients. Agents move toward contexts that promise joy, recognition, belonging and possibility. They move away from contexts that predict shame, exclusion, domination, instability or loss. Research on ostracism and social exclusion supports the claim that exclusion is not merely symbolic; it can produce distress and threaten fundamental needs [Williams, 2007; Eisenberger et al., 2003]. Research on social identity also supports the claim that group membership can carry emotional and evaluative significance [Tajfel & Turner, 1979].

Counterpoint: social identity theory and Durkheimian sociology both caution against reducing group life to individual utility-like gradients. Group membership can shape the self-concept itself, and social facts can exist as inherited, coercive structures before the individual evaluates them [Tajfel & Turner, 1979; Durkheim, 1895].

## 4. Social Emotion as X-Gradient

Social emotions are the felt gradients of social X-fields. They inform the agent whether a social context is opening, closing, validating, disturbing, permitting, denying, repairing or breaking continuation.

In PABEN, a social emotion does not arise from the social situation alone. It arises from a belief evaluated inside a social context: what I can do, what others can do, whether I disturb the shared context, whether others disturb it, whether I am allowed access, whether others are allowed access, and whether I or others lack access to fulfillment.

This gives social emotion a structural form. Each social emotion can be read as a felt change in one component of social X:

- **Executability / Q:** whether action can be carried by self or others
- **Trustability / T:** whether the shared context remains stable or is disturbed
- **Reachability / R:** whether access and continuation are socially permitted
- **Valencability / V:** whether fulfillment-access is present or lacking

*Table 1: Social emotions as gradients of social X. The table maps social emotions onto the four X-components: Executability, Trustability, Reachability and Valencability. Each pair expresses a perspectival relation between self and other. The emotion in parentheses marks the mirrored or complementary position in the same social X-structure. The table should be read as a behavioral-regulatory map: each cell names a recurring position in social X-regulation.*

There are existing formal and semi-formal approaches to emotion. Appraisal theories classify emotions through evaluative dimensions such as goal relevance, responsibility, control and expected outcome. The OCC model formalizes emotions through appraisals of events, agents and objects. Computational appraisal models such as EMA implement appraisal dynamics in artificial agents. Affect Control Theory provides a mathematical account of social interaction, where emotions arise from the confirmation or disconfirmation of culturally shared affective meanings.

The present table is related to these approaches, but it does something different. It does not begin with an existing list of emotions and then ask how they can be classified. It begins with the formal PABEN field: who acts, who disturbs, who is allowed, who lacks, and which X-component is changed. When this field is unfolded, distinct social-emotional positions appear.

This convergence is theoretically significant. The table produces recognizable human social-emotional labels: duty, gratitude, guilt, indignation, shame, anger, pride, envy, humiliation, compassion, loneliness and others. The exact linguistic labels can be discussed, but the positions themselves are not arbitrary: they arise from how social beliefs are carried, blocked, permitted, disturbed or deprived across self and other.

The lived emotional picture can still be complex, but the complexity does not require the underlying emotions to be vague. A shared social field usually contains many active beliefs at once. Several agents may be acting, disturbing, allowing, lacking, judging, repairing or withholding access at the same time. The resulting experience may therefore contain several social emotions simultaneously or in rapid alternation.

For example, a person may feel guilt because their own action damaged a shared context, shame because the self is socially lowered, fear because repair may fail, longing because access to the group remains valuable, and hope or relief if forgiveness becomes possible. The emotional situation is complex because the Belief-field is complex. The individual emotion mechanisms remain structurally specific.

The table therefore does not explain complexity by weakening the emotion labels. It explains complexity by multiplying the number of active beliefs.

The claim of the table is stronger than a loose taxonomy. It proposes that social emotions correspond to recurring mechanical positions in social X-regulation. Social emotion is not merely an inner state. It is the agent's felt registration of changes in access to shared continuation.

The basic social-emotional question is:

> Can I continue as a valid agent in this social context?

## 5. Persons as Social Contexts

The first social context is another person. A person becomes attractive when they increase X: they provide trust, care, recognition, play, knowledge, permission, help, calm or shared joy. A person becomes aversive when they lower X: they produce unpredictability, shame, rejection, domination, confusion or danger.

Attachment research and social baseline theory support the general claim that other people can function as regulators of emotion, effort and security [Ainsworth et al., 1978; Beckes & Coan, 2011]. The need-to-belong hypothesis likewise treats stable relational bonds as a fundamental human motivation [Baumeister & Leary, 1995].

This does not mean social relations are selfish in a narrow sense. The agent may experience another person's joy as part of its own X. Care, love, loyalty and friendship become possible because the other person's continuation is included in the agent's own belief-field. This overlaps with research on close relationships and gratitude, where responsiveness and mutual benefit can strengthen relational bonds [Algoe, 2012].

A person can therefore be:

- an attractor
- an aversive field
- an unstable field
- a provider-context
- a judge-context
- a repair-context
- a belonging-context

The relation becomes stable when both agents repeatedly increase each other's X through recognizable behavior.

## 6. Shared Joy and the Formation of Social Fields

Social life is not driven only by fear, shame or punishment. It is also driven by the search for shared joy. In PABEN terms, joy marks successful positive closure: a route worked, the belief carried, and X increased.

Shared joy is socially powerful because it confirms that a context can carry multiple agents at once. Play, cooperation, celebration, learning, humor, ritual, shared work and mutual recognition all strengthen social contexts by producing repeated positive closure. Durkheim's account of ritual and collective effervescence provides a classic sociological parallel: shared ritual emotion can generate social solidarity and moral force [Durkheim, 1912]. Research on cooperation and shared intentionality also supports the idea that humans are especially adapted for joint action and shared goals [Tomasello, 2009].

The core mechanism is:

```text
repeated shared X-increase
-> social attraction
-> expectation
-> norm
-> group
-> institution
```

A group does not emerge because a higher organism absorbs individuals. A group emerges because multiple agents repeatedly evaluate the same shared context as an attractor and regulate their behavior to preserve access to it.

Counterpoint: Durkheim and Searle would push against a purely bottom-up version of this claim. Once formed, social facts and institutional facts can become external to any one agent and can constrain agents who did not create them [Durkheim, 1895; Searle, 1995].

## 7. Groups as Shared X-Attractors

A group is a shared X-attractor. It forms when several agents experience the same social context as valuable, reachable, executable and trustworthy enough to preserve.

In PABEN language:

> A group is a stabilized social context that multiple agents evaluate as an attractor for continuation.

Groups stabilize:

- belonging
- shared action
- shared memory
- mutual recognition
- protection
- permission
- identity
- repair
- shared joy

Social identity theory strongly supports the idea that group membership is not merely external membership, but can become part of self-understanding and social evaluation [Tajfel & Turner, 1979]. Research on belonging also supports the idea that group membership can carry affective and motivational force [Baumeister & Leary, 1995].

But a group can also become aversive. It may demand behavior that lowers individual X, block repair, produce shame, punish difference or preserve obsolete beliefs. The same group can be an attractor for some agents and an aversive field for others.

The group question is:

> Does this "we" increase or decrease my possibility of valid continuation?

## 8. Norms as Stabilized Emotional Expectations

Norms emerge when repeated social X-evaluations become expected. A norm is not only a rule. It is a stabilized emotional expectation about which behavior will preserve or lower access to the shared context.

| Norm function | PABEN interpretation |
|---|---|
| "Do this" | This behavior preserves shared X |
| "Do not do this" | This behavior threatens shared X |
| "You may do this" | This route is socially permitted |
| "You owe this" | Others' continuation depends on your behavior |
| "You must repair this" | A damaged shared route must be restored |

This account is close to Bicchieri's view of social norms as expectation-dependent: norms are sustained by beliefs about what others do and what others think one ought to do [Bicchieri, 2006]. It is also compatible with evidence that punishment can sustain cooperation, although the motives behind punishment are contested [Fehr & Gachter, 2002; Fowler et al., 2005].

Norms become powerful because they allow agents to predict social emotion. A norm tells the agent where pride, gratitude, guilt, shame, indignation, forgiveness or exclusion may appear.

Counterpoint: some norm theories emphasize cognition, expectation and coordination more than emotion. Others emphasize material incentives, sanctions, institutional enforcement or cultural transmission. The PABEN interpretation should therefore be read as an affective-regulatory layer, not as a complete theory of norms [Bicchieri, 2006; Ostrom, 1990].

## 9. Social Organization

Social organization emerges when agents begin to coordinate around someone or something that can move the shared belief-field. A group does not always know what to do. It may feel threat, uncertainty, opportunity, conflict or need without having a stable shared Belief that can carry action. In such situations, social organization requires that someone does something.

The first form of organization is not domination. It is granted collective agency. An agent acts in a way that increases X for others. If the action opens a viable route, others experience a positive social update: relief, gratitude, admiration, respect, shared joy or pride-by-association. The acting agent is then marked as someone who can carry collective behavior.

In PABEN terms:

```text
action that increases others' X
-> gratitude / admiration / relief / shared joy
-> honor
-> trust
-> permission to lead
-> ability to move the shared belief-field
```

This is one possible emotional root of authority. We honor those who increase our X. A caregiver, teacher, healer, reformer, protector, representative, scientist, political leader or moral exemplar can gain authority when others experience their behavior as route-opening. They do not merely possess power; they are granted social permission through repeated recognition.

Classical sociology gives a broader account. Weber distinguishes forms of legitimate authority such as traditional, charismatic and rational-legal authority; PABEN's honored X-increase maps most naturally onto the emotional basis of charismatic or earned authority, but it does not fully explain legal-bureaucratic legitimacy or inherited authority [Weber, 1978].

In this sense:

> Power is accumulated social permission produced by honored X-increase.

The socially organized agent becomes a provider-context for others' Beliefs:

```text
X_social(B | C_representative)
```

The representative, leader or authority figure becomes attractive when their presence makes valuable Beliefs more reachable, executable, trustworthy and need-relieving. They can move the collective field because others allow their actions, words or judgments to reorganize what the group experiences as possible.

This also explains representation. A group often needs someone to act on behalf of the shared belief-field. The representative condenses collective uncertainty into a carried Belief: "we do this now." If the action succeeds, the group experiences shared positive closure. The representative is honored because their behavior allowed the group to continue.

Social organization becomes dangerous when the granted permission remains after the X-increase disappears. Authority can detach from lived consequence. The same structure that once opened routes can begin to block repair, demand loyalty, suppress correction or preserve the status of the representative instead of the viability of the shared field.

## 10. Institutions as Stored Social X-Regulation

Institutions emerge when social X-regulation becomes durable across time. A school, court, currency, profession, archive, ritual system or legal order stores solutions to repeated social behavior problems.

In PABEN terms:

> Institutions are stored social Experience: retained solutions for stabilizing social X across many agents and episodes.

This formulation is compatible with institutional theories that treat institutions as durable rule systems for coordinating repeated problems, especially in collective-action settings [Ostrom, 1990]. It also overlaps with Searle's view that institutional facts depend on collective intentionality and shared status-functions [Searle, 1995].

An institution works when it increases social X by making routes more trustworthy, executable, reachable and valuable. It fails when it lowers X by becoming opaque, humiliating, inaccessible, corrupt, obsolete or hostile to lived consequence.

Examples:

| Institution | X-function |
|---|---|
| School | Makes learning routes reachable and socially recognized |
| Court | Stabilizes conflict repair through procedure |
| Currency | Makes exchange executable among strangers |
| Healthcare | Makes bodily threat socially reachable for repair |
| Science | Stabilizes shared correction of claims |
| Archive | Preserves collective memory |

An institution can be an attractor, an aversive field or both, depending on the agent's position inside the social X-field.

Counterpoint: institutions cannot be reduced to stored emotion. They also include rules, material resources, enforcement mechanisms, role systems, records, technologies and legal structures [Ostrom, 1990; Searle, 1995].

## 11. Society as Overlapping Social X-Fields

Society is not one organism with one will. Society is an overlapping field of social X-evaluations. It consists of many agents orienting toward and away from persons, groups, institutions, roles, public scenes, moral orders and future possibilities.

In PABEN language:

> Society is a stabilized ecology of shared X-fields.

Society becomes stable when many agents can increase X through the same network of contexts. It becomes unstable when the network increases X for some agents by lowering it for others, or when institutions no longer match lived consequence.

Social conflict can then be described as conflict between X-fields:

- one group experiences a structure as an attractor
- another experiences the same structure as aversive
- one institution preserves one group's Reachability
- another group's routes are blocked
- one norm produces pride for some and shame for others
- one sacred order gives belonging to some and exclusion to others

This makes social conflict a problem of shared regulation:

> Whose X is being increased, whose X is being lowered, and which contexts are being protected as attractive?

Counterpoint: this framing risks making social conflict too psychological. Material inequality, coercion, law, historical inheritance, technology, ecology and economic production may shape social fields before agents evaluate them. The PABEN account should therefore be treated as a mechanism for social-emotional regulation, not as a complete sociology.

| Emergent form | Generated by | Stabilizes | Can deform into |

| Social emotion | Felt change in social X | Correction, belonging, repair | Shame spirals, resentment |
| Norm | Repeated emotional expectation | Predictable behavior | Moral rigidity |
| Group | Shared X-attractor | Belonging, identity, shared action | Exclusion, conformity pressure |
| Social organization | Honored X-increase by route-opening agents | Collective action and representation | Detached authority, loyalty pressure |
| Institution | Stored social X-regulation | Long-term coordination | Bureaucratic aversion, corruption |
| Society | Overlapping shared X-fields | Large-scale continuation | Structural conflict |

## 13. Limits

This study is a conceptual hypothesis. It does not replace sociology, social psychology, anthropology or religious studies. It offers a PABEN grammar for linking behavior, social emotion and emergent social structure.

The main risks are:

- overextending X as an explanatory variable
- underestimating material conditions
- making social behavior too individualistic
- treating all social structures as emotional products
- overlooking history, culture and language
- overgeneralizing from WEIRD psychological research [Henrich et al., 2010]
- treating institutions as only affective rather than also legal, material and symbolic [Ostrom, 1990; Searle, 1995]

The corrective is to keep the claim modest:

> Social X-fields are not the whole of social life. They are a proposed mechanism for how individual social emotion can scale into stable shared regulation.

## 14. Conclusion

The central proposal is:

> Social structures emerge from repeated individual X-evaluations of social contexts.

Agents move toward people, groups, institutions and sacred orders that increase X. They avoid, resist or repair contexts that lower X. Social emotions are the felt gradients of this movement. Shared joy, recognition, gratitude, belonging and forgiveness pull agents into stable fields. Shame, guilt, fear, humiliation, indignation and exclusion mark threatened or broken access.

Groups, norms, institutions and religions are therefore not higher organisms that replace the individual. They are emergent social fields created by many individuals trying to stabilize and increase continuation together.

In PABEN terms, society is not above behavior. Society is what repeated socially felt behavior becomes when many agents seek higher X inside shared contexts.

This is the PABEN claim. The strongest external support comes from research on belonging, social regulation, social pain, moral emotions, norms and cooperation. The strongest external challenge comes from sociology, institutional theory and cultural psychology: social structures may become external, coercive, historically inherited and culturally variable in ways that cannot be fully explained from present individual evaluation alone [Durkheim, 1895; Ostrom, 1990; Searle, 1995; Henrich et al., 2010].
