## 1. Purpose and Core Hypothesis

This deep dive asks whether the functional domains of the PABEN model can be plausibly situated within known brain anatomy and information flow. The decisive question is not whether each domain can be reduced to one isolated "center", but whether the model's functional roles fit larger cortico-subcortical loops.

The working hypothesis is that the PABEN domains correspond to functional roles in recurrent circuits. Posterior neocortex provides context. Frontal and motor neocortex carry skill and action. Hippocampal and entorhinal formations carry experience, episode, transition, and continuity. An amygdala-centered but broader interoceptive-affective network carries need pressure, valence, salience, and relief/pain relevance. The basal ganglia-thalamus system gates belief and continuation.

A central model feature is that P/Context and A/Skill are understood as symmetric neocortical hierarchies. The context side and the skill side are both layered, abstraction-bearing structures that can be bound through B and automated through repeated C-S use.

There are four important reservations. First, this is not a localist model: the domains are circuit roles. Second, P/A symmetry is not evidence in itself, but a model-architectural design principle that must be assessed by its alignment with the hierarchical and laminar organization of cortex. Third, "belief" is not a propositional sentence. In the current PABEN vocabulary, a Belief is an actionable C-S continuation, supported by prior E episodes and opened under N pressure: "I can do something here, and this has carried continuation before." Fourth, learning is not limited to a narrow Hebbian rule, but must be reducible to biologically plausible coactivation-, timing-, state-, and neuromodulator-dependent plasticity.

| PABEN domain | Anatomical working hypothesis | Functional reading |
|---|---|---|
| P / C | Posterior neocortex: occipital, temporal, parietal; layered context hierarchy | Actual context, perception, affordance, world-state; hierarchical C side |
| A / S | Frontal neocortex: motor cortex, premotor cortex, SMA, PFC; layered skill/action hierarchy | Skill hierarchy, action, intended/possible action; hierarchical S side |
| B | Basal ganglia plus thalamus in cortico-BG-thalamo-cortical loops | Belief-gating: opening, closing, and selecting C-S continuations |
| E | Hippocampus, entorhinal cortex, and parahippocampal regions | Experience, episode, transition, continuity, relational topology |
| N | Amygdala region plus interoception, hypothalamus, brainstem, and insula | Need pressure, valence, salience, relief/pain history, fulfillment relevance |

## 2. General Neurobiological Landscape: Loops Rather Than Modules

Recent neurobiological thinking rarely supports simple linear pipelines. Perception, action, memory, valence, and selection are organized in recurrent circuits where cortex, hippocampus, basal ganglia, thalamus, amygdala/interoception, and neuromodulatory systems continuously influence one another. This is important for PABEN because the model should not be read as a box diagram of independent modules.

From a PABEN perspective, biological alignment is strongest if the model is read as loop matching: P, A, B, E, and N are functional roles in a circuit where actual context, possible skill, remembered continuity, bodily-affective pressure, and gated continuation co-produce action and learning.

| PABEN Position |
|---|
| The biology of PABEN is not module placement, but loop matching. P recognizes actual context; A carries skill; B gates continuation; E binds episodes and transitions; N colors pressure and valence; plasticity stabilizes the coactivations that can be carried. |

## 3. P/C: Posterior Neocortex as Context and Affordance System

### What the Literature Says

Posterior neocortex, including occipital, temporal, and parietal cortex, is not merely raw sensation. Occipital cortex is central for visual processing. Temporal cortex contributes to object, auditory, language, and semantic processing. Parietal cortex contributes to spatial processing, multisensory integration, attention, and action-relevant spatial organization.

The affordance literature is especially relevant because it connects perception with action possibilities. Sakreida et al. (2016) found in a meta-analysis that affordances are processed in segregated parieto-frontal dorsal pathways, with stable and variable affordances having partly distinct anatomical profiles.

The dorsal stream literature also supports the idea that perception for action is not merely a late motor addition. Freud et al. (2020) argue that dorsal cortex contributes to several visuospatial computations relevant to both perception and action. Affordance studies similarly show that object perception often involves motor areas, not visual areas alone (Maranesi et al., 2014).

### How PABEN Relates to the Theme

This aligns well with the model's placement of P/C in posterior neocortex. In PABEN, perception is not just sensory input, but actual context: the situation the agent is actually in, organized so that it can meet skill. P/C should therefore not be understood as passive registration, but as recognition of an affordance-relevant world.

The PABEN principle that perception provides actuality rather than error also fits here. Posterior cortex can be understood as an active constructor of the current world/context-state. Error or mismatch arises only relative to an open Belief or expectation, not as perception's primary product.

The alignment is strong, but P/C should not be isolated too narrowly. Context in PABEN is broader than posterior perception alone. Social meaning, memory, affective salience, and task set all influence which contexts become relevant. P/C should therefore be read as a posterior context front that is continuously modulated by E, N, B, and A.

## 4. A/S: Frontal Neocortex as Skill and Action Hierarchy

### What the Literature Says

Motor and premotor areas organize concrete action, but newer theories also place prefrontal cortex in a broader action-control perspective. Badre and Nee (2018) describe frontal cortex as organized around hierarchical cognitive control. Fine and Hayden (2022) go further and argue that the whole prefrontal cortex can be understood as fundamentally premotor: an action abstraction hierarchy that potentiates and depotentiates possible action plans at different levels of abstraction.

This literature makes it possible to see frontal cortex as more than an executive command center. It can be understood as a hierarchy of action possibilities, where higher layers are not necessarily "pure thought", but more abstract forms of action selection, task maintenance, and goal-directed control.

### How PABEN Relates to the Theme

This is a very strong alignment with the A/S domain. In the model, skill is not only muscle movement. Skill ranges from concrete motor patterns to abstract action readiness, strategy, regulation, and social action. If PFC can be read as an action abstraction hierarchy, it fits directly with the idea that S extends from motor cortex to PFC.

PABEN thereby avoids making PFC a separate planner. PFC becomes the high end of the skill hierarchy: the place where more abstract, longer, and more context-sensitive skills can be carried. This fits the PABEN idea that execution is not sequence playback, but continuous context-skill zipping.

The alignment depends on "skill" being understood broadly, but not without limits. Within PABEN, action is not only motor output. Waiting, regulating, speaking, choosing, imagining, and following a rule are also skill forms. Biological alignment therefore depends on whether PFC can legitimately be read as an action/control hierarchy rather than as a symbolic central processor. The Fine and Hayden line makes that reading plausible.

## 5. P/A Symmetry and Neocortical Layering as a Model Feature

### What the Literature Says

Neocortex is organized in layers and hierarchies. Classical connectional literature describes distributed hierarchical processing in primate cortex, especially in visual and multimodal cortical networks (Felleman & Van Essen, 1991). Laminar and microcircuit literature likewise describes neocortex as built from repeated but locally specialized circuits, where input, local processing, and output are organized through layered connections (Douglas & Martin, 2004; Bastos et al., 2012).

The relation between sensory and motor cortical areas is not a simple symmetric copy. Motor cortex is agranular or dysgranular in ways that distinguish it from classical granular sensory cortex. Shipp (2005) describes these differences and gradual transitions between visual and motor cortex, emphasizing the importance of agranular architecture. Symmetry should therefore not be understood as anatomical identity, but as functional and hierarchical compatibility between the perception/context side and the action/skill side.

### How PABEN Relates to the Theme

PABEN assumes a structural symmetry between P/C and A/S. Perception/Context and Action/Skill are understood as mirrored neocortical hierarchies: P/C organizes the world as recognizable context structures, while A/S organizes action as carriable skill structures. This symmetry is not evidence for the model in itself; it is a design principle and a model feature.

The model point is that B does not have to connect a flat perception with a flat motor response. B can bind context and skill structures at corresponding abstraction levels: "I can do something here." This reading makes C-S bindings more biologically plausible because both the C side and the S side are assumed to be hierarchical, layered, and neocortical.

At the same time, the biological asymmetry must be preserved. P/C and A/S are model-symmetric, but cortex is not anatomically identical on both sides. The symmetry is functional: both sides can carry hierarchies, both can be activated top-down and bottom-up in loops, and both can be connected through repeated Belief use. Differences in laminar architecture are therefore not a weakening of the model, but a biological precision: context and skill hierarchies can be compatible without being identical.

The consequence for B is important. The basal ganglia-thalamus system should not be understood as the place where the whole content of a Belief is stored. The content is carried by the neocortical C and S hierarchies, and by E/N history. The B system gates or opens a structured C-S continuation. This makes the Belief concept less localist and more circuit-oriented.

## 6. E: Hippocampal and Entorhinal Formation as Experience and Continuity

### What the Literature Says

The hippocampus literature connects this region with episodic memory, relational memory, navigation, cognitive maps, generalization, and replay. Eichenbaum (2014, 2017) argues that the role of hippocampus in navigation follows from its broader function as relational organization of memory. Epstein et al. (2017) describe cognitive maps in humans as spatial and non-spatial representational forms that can support memory and guide future action. Garvert et al. (2023) show that hippocampal formation can organize relations between events in cognitive maps relevant for generalization and inference.

Entorhinal cortex is central as an interface between neocortex and hippocampus. Memory consolidation literature describes cortical-hippocampal-cortical loops where hippocampal replay and cortical activity can influence one another over time. Rothschild et al. (2017), for example, show a cortical-hippocampal-cortical loop during consolidation, where cortical activity can influence hippocampal replay, which then influences cortical networks.

### How PABEN Relates to the Theme

This is one of the model's strongest anatomical alignments. E is not "memory" as passive storage, but transitional continuity: what tends to follow from what, in which context, through which Belief, and with which consequence. This fits well with hippocampus/entorhinal formation as a relational, episodic, and map-based system.

Importantly, E is not the only structure that carries all experience forever. In PABEN, direct C-S connections can be established through repeated Belief use. Well-learned perception-action couplings can therefore shift weight from hippocampal/episodic handling toward more direct cortical C-S execution. Hippocampal and entorhinal formation are especially plausible as active transitional and episodic continuity systems, while stable C-S couplings can be consolidated and automated cortically.

This corrects a possible misunderstanding: PABEN does not say that all learning remains in E. E contributes sequence, transition, episode, and continuity. Repeated use can establish more direct C-S-like connections. This fits consolidation literature and the distinction between new/unstable learning and automated Recognize-Execute flow.

## 7. B: Basal Ganglia and Thalamus as Belief-Gating

### What the Literature Says

The basal ganglia are part of cortico-basal ganglia-thalamocortical loops connecting cortex, striatum, pallidum, thalamus, and cortex again. These loops are not only motoric; they also include associative, limbic, oculomotor, and prefrontal circuits. Humphries et al. (2006) model the basal ganglia as a physiologically plausible action selection system. Schroll and Hamker (2013) review computational models of basal ganglia pathway functions and show how the direct, indirect, and hyperdirect pathways are often understood in relation to selection, inhibition, and control.

The O'Reilly and Munakata tradition describes basal ganglia action selection as operating across a broad range of frontal cortical areas, including motor, oculomotor, prefrontal, orbitofrontal, and anterior cingulate loops. Newer work even attempts to connect hippocampal cognitive maps with interacting cortico-basal ganglia-thalamocortical loops (Hamker et al., 2025).

### How PABEN Relates to the Theme

This is the most important and most terminologically sensitive mapping. B should not be read as "belief" in the ordinary propositional sense. Biologically, B is better understood as a gated continuation: a C-S structure supported by E episodes and colored by N pressure, saying "I can do something here, and this continuation has a particular experiential and affective history." This is precisely why the word Belief remains fitting: not as sentence-belief, but as an actionable expectation that something can be carried from here.

In this reading, the basal ganglia do not store the whole Belief by themselves. The neocortical P/C and A/S hierarchies carry the context and skill content. Hippocampal and entorhinal formation contribute E-continuity. N systems contribute need pressure, affective urgency, and valence. The basal ganglia-thalamus system gates, opens, and stabilizes which structured C-S continuation is allowed to carry behavior.

This makes B as basal ganglia plus thalamus plausible if B is understood as belief-gating rather than belief-storage. In that reading, thalamus can function as re-entry or enablement back into cortex, while basal ganglia selectively disinhibit or inhibit competing loops. P/A symmetry is important here because B gates the connection between two hierarchical cortical sides, not between raw sensation and raw motor output.

| Terminological Protection |
|---|
| Belief is a dangerous word because in ordinary language it means propositional conviction. In PABEN, a Belief is a carriable C-S continuation supported by E/N history: "I can do something here." That is exactly why the word is also beautiful: Belief is not a sentence, but a portable possibility. |

## 8. N: Amygdala Region and Broader Interoceptive-Affective Function

### What the Literature Says

The amygdala is associated with threat, salience, learned affective significance, and emotion-related prioritization, but modern affective neuroscience places emotion and valence in broader networks. The interoception literature is central. Khalsa et al. (2018) describe interoceptive signaling as closely linked to reflexes, urges, feelings, drives, adaptive responses, cognitive and emotional experiences, body regulation, and survival. Feldman et al. (2024) argue that understanding affect requires understanding information flow through interoceptive pathways.

Insula, anterior cingulate, hypothalamus, brainstem, autonomic regulation, endocrine systems, and amygdala all participate in the body's and brain's regulatory landscape. It is therefore biologically risky to reduce Need or Fulfillment to the amygdala alone.

### How PABEN Relates to the Theme

The PABEN hypothesis should therefore formulate N as an amygdala-centered but broader interoceptive-affective function. The key claim is not that there is one narrow anatomical center, but that the brain has regions and circuits that deliver the N function: bodily pressure, valence, salience, relief/pain history, urgency, and affective prioritization.

This aligns with modern interoception and affect literature. N is actual need pressure and the felt fulfillment profile that makes a situation matter. It requires access to bodily regulation and affective valuation. The amygdala region is important, but not sufficient alone. The best biological formulation is therefore that N is realized by an interoceptive-affective network with the amygdala as a central node, not as the whole system.

This precision strengthens the model because it avoids a localist overclaim. PABEN only needs to claim that there is a biological N function that can color and prioritize Beliefs with pressure, valence, and relief/pain relevance.

## 9. Hebbian and Coactivation-Based Learning: Necessary, but Not Narrow

### What the Literature Says

Hebbian learning is often summarized as "cells that fire together wire together", but modern biological plasticity is much richer. Spike-timing-dependent plasticity (STDP) is a concrete timing-based extension of Hebbian learning. Dan and Poo (2004) review STDP as a timing-dependent synaptic learning rule, and Caporale and Dan (2008) describe STDP as a Hebbian learning rule demonstrated in many circuits and species.

At the same time, the literature shows that plasticity is neuromodulatory and state-dependent. Fremaux and Gerstner (2016) describe neuromodulated STDP and reinforcement learning. In addition, there are inhibitory plasticity, homeostatic plasticity, synaptic tagging, consolidation, and sleep/replay processes. The biological learning landscape therefore cannot be reduced to one simple Hebbian rule.

### How PABEN Relates to the Theme

PABEN should therefore not say that all learning processes are Hebbian in a narrow sense. The stronger and more biologically durable formulation is that all PABEN inferences and learning processes must be realizable through coactivation-, timing-, state-, and neuromodulator-dependent plasticity. This remains Hebbian in the architectural sense: no stable binding without relevant coactivation.

This fits the PABEN principle "no synchrony, no binding", but it requires gating. Pure coactivation would bind too much. Attention, arousal, N pressure, basal ganglia-thalamus gating, repetition, sleep/replay, and consolidation must therefore determine which coactivations are actually stabilized.

The model's strength is that it does not require biologically unrealistic global error backpropagation. Its challenge is to specify which coactivation events are allowed to become learning. Here, N, B-gating, and E-continuity are decisive.

## 10. Reflection as Biological Internal Coactivation: S -> C -> E Loop

### What the Literature Says

Reflection, planning, and imagination are biologically harder to situate than perception and action, but hippocampal replay and prefrontal-hippocampal interaction are relevant. Olafsdottir et al. (2018) review hippocampal replay in memory and planning and emphasize that replay can support both memory consolidation and adaptive decisions. Jensen et al. (2024) propose a recurrent network model where planning is implemented through prefrontal-hippocampal interactions, and where hippocampal replays are both triggered by and influence prefrontal dynamics.

The field is not unambiguous. Replay can support consolidation, map-building, retrospective evaluation, prospective planning, or several functions at the same time. Recent discussions emphasize that replay should not be reduced to a single function.

### How PABEN Relates to the Theme

The distinctive PABEN contribution is the attempt to make Reflection Hebbian/coactivation-based. Reflection is not symbolic manipulation in a separate space. It is internal coactivation where a possible skill direction can activate possible contexts, which are then tested against E-continuity and N relevance.

The hypothetical biological form is this: A/PFC activates a possible skill or action abstract; this activation recruits relevant C/affordance contexts in posterior cortex; hippocampal and entorhinal formation activate relational transitions and possible continuations; N colors the configuration with pressure and valence; basal ganglia-thalamus gates the candidate continuation that can be opened as a Belief.

The important point is that this can still be plasticity-based. When C, S, E, and N are held active in an inner now, a new candidate binding can stabilize. Reflection is therefore not an exception to the learning principle, but an internal way of creating the coactivations that learning requires.

| PABEN Reading of Reflection |
|---|
| Reflection is a backward hook-finding loop: S -> C -> E. The system does not symbolically ask "what should I do?", but activates possible skills, recruits contexts, tests continuity, and opens a carriable C-S continuation if it can stabilize. |

## 11. Direct C-S Connections and Automation

### What the Literature Says

Perception-action literature and affordance literature show that perception and action are tightly coupled. Affordance processing involves parieto-frontal networks, and object perception can activate motor systems. Consolidation literature also shows that repeated experiences can gradually change hippocampal dependency and strengthen cortical relations.

### How PABEN Relates to the Theme

This is central for PABEN: repeated Belief use establishes direct C-S connections. E is not a permanent mediator for everything that has ever been learned. When a Belief has been used stably enough, C and S can become more directly coupled, allowing Recognize-Execute to run faster and more automatically.

Biologically, this means that new, unstable, episodic, or reflective action is expected to depend more on hippocampal/entorhinal E and prefrontal-BG gating, while well-learned C-S couplings can be carried more directly in cortico-cortical and cortico-subcortical loops. This fits the model's distinction between what is stable enough to execute and what must still be held in Reflection or the horizon.

## 12. Processual Flows: Try-Observe, Reflection, and Recognize-Execute

### Why Flows Are as Important as Domains

Domain mapping indicates where the model's functional roles may be anatomically situated. But PABEN also requires that the same brain systems can be coupled in different processual flow regimes. It is therefore not enough that P/C, A/S, B, E, and N can be identified as plausible circuit roles. The model must also align with biological flows where perception-action, internal simulation, and stable execution shift in dominance.

The three central PABEN processes are Try-Observe, Reflection, and Recognize-Execute. They should not be understood as three separate modules, but as three ways the same cortico-subcortical systems can run: an open external learning flow, an internal simulating hook-finding flow, and a stable gated execution flow.

### What the Literature Says

The literature on active sensing and action-perception loops emphasizes that perception is not passive registration. Organisms collect information through action, and action changes the data from which the system learns. Yang, Wolpert, and Lengyel (2016) describe active sensing as a closed loop between observation and action, and Little and Sommer (2013) emphasize that behavior becomes a critical determinant of learning when data are gathered in action-perception loops.

For internal simulation and planning, hippocampal-prefrontal replay is especially relevant. Shin et al. (2019) describe a learning-related shift from hippocampal reverse replay-based retrospective evaluation to forward replay-based prospective planning. Jensen et al. (2024) propose a recurrent network model where planning can be implemented through prefrontal-hippocampal interactions, with hippocampal replay both triggered by and influencing prefrontal dynamics.

For stable execution, cortico-basal ganglia-thalamocortical loops are central. The basal ganglia are often described as involved in action selection, and newer work attempts to understand goal-directed and habitual behavior as interactions between multiple cortex-basal ganglia-thalamus loops rather than as entirely separate systems. This fits the idea that learned C-S connections can become faster and more automatic through repeated use.

### How PABEN Relates to the Theme

Try-Observe corresponds in PABEN to an open perception-action-learning flow, but should more precisely be understood as variant formation in both the S and C hierarchies. The agent does not merely test a finished skill against a finished context. The skill itself can become more differentiated, and the context itself can become more precise through the observed consequence.

Initially, the C-S connection is probably B-mediated: an open Belief holds the relation between "what this is" and "what I can do here" active long enough for coactivation to stabilize new C and S variants. Through repeated successful use, the relation can consolidate into more direct C-S connections, later enabling fast Recognize-Execute without full B/E mediation.

Reflection corresponds to an internal simulating flow. Instead of waiting for world feedback, the system attempts to hold possible S, C, E, and N configurations active in an inner now. The distinctive PABEN thesis is that Reflection can remain plasticity-based: hypotheses are made through a backward S -> C -> E/N loop, where skill hypotheses recruit possible contexts and experiential topologies. If the configuration becomes stable enough, it can bind as a candidate Belief.

Recognize-Execute corresponds to the stable execution flow. Actual context is recognized, a learned C-S continuation is opened or gated, and the skill hierarchy can carry action without reflective hand-holding. Repeated Belief use can establish direct C-S connections, so E no longer has to function as an active mediator for every execution.

### Zip Execution and S -> B Feedback

Zip execution is a necessary precision of Recognize-Execute. In PABEN, execution is not a one-way movement from B to S. An open Belief prepares and gates a skill, but the carried skill must also be able to report back to the Belief system while actual context unfolds. Otherwise the system cannot know whether it is still in the same Belief, whether the skill is still being carried, or whether the Belief should close, adjust, or interrupt.

Biologically, this points to an S -> B component in the loop. Frontal/motor skill activity must be able to influence basal ganglia-thalamic gating back toward cortical loops. This fits cortico-basal ganglia-thalamocortical circuits, where cortex does not merely receive gating from basal ganglia/thalamus, but also continuously sends cortical action/skill-state into the loops that select and maintain action. Zip execution can therefore be read as an ongoing coupling between actual context, carried skill, and open Belief: context unlocks skill; skill changes world/context; skill-state feeds back to B; B maintains or closes continuation.

This S -> B feedback is important because it makes execution dynamic rather than sequential. A Belief does not open a finished route; it keeps a C-S continuation active while the world supplies the next context. Zip execution is therefore not merely a psychological principle, but a biological flow requirement: the action hierarchy must remain in feedback contact with the gating system during execution itself.

Play should not be introduced as a fourth flow in this biological reading. Play is a consequence-weakened frame in which Try-Observe, Reflection, and Recognize-Execute can unfold with reduced reality binding. Play can be physical testing, internal fantasy, social rule invention, or stable execution within an invented rule world. N does not disappear; it still monitors the boundary toward pain, danger, shame, or social loss.

Processual alignment is therefore an important supplement to anatomical alignment. PABEN fits neurobiology best if the brain is understood as a system that can shift flow-mode through overlapping circuits, rather than as a set of separate functional boxes.

## 13. Overall Flow: PABEN as an Anatomical Loop Hypothesis

PABEN's biological flow can be summarized without making the model linear:

- Posterior neocortex: actual context and affordance-relevant world-state; layered P/C hierarchy.
- Frontal/motor neocortex: skill hierarchies from concrete motor patterns to abstract action/control; layered A/S hierarchy.
- Basal ganglia-thalamus: gating, opening, and closing of C-S continuations between symmetric neocortical hierarchies; also receives A/S feedback during zip execution.
- Hippocampal/entorhinal formation: experience, continuity, relational maps, transitions, and replay.
- N network: interoceptive-affective pressure, valence, relief/pain history, fulfillment relevance, and salience.
- Plasticity: coactivation-, timing-, state-, and neuromodulator-dependent stabilization of connections.

Processually, the same systems can shift between Try-Observe, Reflection, and Recognize-Execute: an external variant-forming learning flow, an internal simulation flow, and a stable gated execution flow. Try-Observe is not merely testing, but B-mediated formation of C and S variants that can become direct C-S couplings through repetition. Recognize-Execute must also be understood as zip execution: actual context unlocks carried skill, skill changes world/context, and A/S state feeds back to B so the open Belief can be carried, adjusted, or closed. This flow triad matters because it describes how the model moves through anatomy, not only where its domains can be placed.

In this reading, PABEN aligns well with the brain's documented circuit form. The model fits less well if one demands one-to-one localization of domains, and better if one accepts functional roles in loops.

## 15. Remaining Issues and Precision Needs

- Belief concept: should be consistently defined as an actionable C-S continuation supported by E/N history and gated by B, not as propositional thought.
- N anatomy: should be described as a functional region/network, not as amygdala alone.
- Plasticity: Hebbian is too narrow; the model must specify how coactivation, timing, neuromodulation, arousal, and consolidation work together.
- Reflection loop: the S -> C -> E hypothesis must be made more anatomically precise: which PFC areas, which hippocampal loops, which thalamic gating?
- C-S automation: the model should clearly distinguish E-dependent new/unstable continuation from direct C-S couplings after repeated Belief use.
- P/A symmetry: should be described as functional and hierarchical compatibility, not anatomical identity between posterior and frontal cortex.
- Processual flows: Try-Observe, Reflection, and Recognize-Execute should be linked more precisely to measurable flow regimes. Try-Observe should especially be described as B-mediated variant formation in the S and C hierarchies, Reflection as internal S -> C -> E/N hook-finding, and Recognize-Execute as stable execution through gated or direct C-S couplings.
- Operationalization: T/Q/R/V, coverage, Belief opening, and E-continuity should eventually be linked to measurable behavioral and neurobiological variables.
- Zip execution: S -> B feedback should be specified biologically. The model assumes that skill/action-state can continuously influence Belief-gating, so execution can continue, adjust, or close without becoming a static sequence plan.

## 16. Conclusion

The PABEN model is biologically most plausible if its domains are understood as functional roles in recurrent cortico-subcortical loops. Posterior neocortex provides actual context. Frontal/motor cortex carries skill and action hierarchy. P/C and A/S form model-symmetric, layered neocortical hierarchies. Basal ganglia-thalamus gates which C-S continuations are opened as Beliefs. Hippocampal and entorhinal formation carry experience, relational continuity, and replay. N is realized in a broad amygdala-centered interoceptive-affective function. Learning occurs through coactivation-, timing-, state-, and neuromodulator-dependent plasticity.

Beyond domain alignment, the model should also be assessed by flow alignment. Try-Observe, Reflection, and Recognize-Execute describe three flow regimes through overlapping circuits: variant formation, internal hook-finding, and stable zip execution. Zip execution adds that execution does not only move B -> S; carried skill must also feed back S -> B, so Belief-gating can follow current action and close or continue the right continuation.

The overall alignment is good, especially for P/C, A/S, P/A symmetry as a model feature, E, and direct C-S automation. The most sensitive part is B, because "Belief" is easily misunderstood. But if Belief is understood as "I can do something here", a carriable C-S continuation supported by E/N history, then basal ganglia-thalamus as belief-gating becomes a strong and elegant anatomical working hypothesis.

This biological deep dive therefore does not show that PABEN has been proven. It shows that the model's structure is in reasonable alignment with the brain's documented loop architecture, and that its next biological task is to specify gating, plasticity, and the Reflection loop in greater detail.
