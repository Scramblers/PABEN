# Developmental Research and the PABEN Model

This review examines recent and central developmental research relevant to the PABEN model. The purpose is not to prove the model from the literature, but to map what the field currently emphasizes, which phenomena recur across the field, and how the PABEN model relates to those phenomena if the model is assumed.

First: what does the field say? Then: how would PABEN understand this?

The direction of the argument is therefore not "developmental research proves PABEN." The direction is: developmental research describes the phenomena; PABEN offers one possible mechanical reading of them.

## 1. General Trend in Developmental Research

Recent developmental research largely understands infant development as relational, bodily, dynamic, and context-dependent. The child is not treated as a small isolated information processor, but as an active system that develops through interaction between bodily needs, perception, action, caregiver response, social timing, environmental stability, motor exploration, emotional co-regulation, and gradual self-regulation (Ilyka et al., 2021; Wass et al., 2024).

Systematic reviews of infant social interaction and brain development suggest that social exchanges in infancy are considered critical for optimal development, and that research increasingly tries to connect observable adult-child interactions with later brain measures and developmental outcomes (Ilyka et al., 2021). Newer co-regulation models likewise describe child and caregiver as a regulatory system in which body, brain, and relation participate in dynamic feedback loops (Wass et al., 2024).

The central research picture is not that the child simply matures from within. It is rather that the child matures through repeated bodily and socially organized regulatory sequences.

**PABEN comment:** This is close to the core intuition of the PABEN model: development is not the addition of new cognitive mechanisms, but the stabilization of the same basic structures through experience. In PABEN language, development is the movement from thin, unstable continuations toward denser belief-blankets, more precise E closures, and a wider Belief-horizon: a larger set of known, reachable, and expectation-bearing routes from the current situation into possible continuations. Genetics, nutrition, sleep, maturation, sensory systems, stress biology, and culture can strongly affect development, but at the cognitive level described by PABEN they do not introduce separate cognitive mechanisms. They affect the conditions, speed, thresholds, and stability of the same mechanism.

## 2. Responsive Care and "Serve-and-Return"

### What the Field Says

A central theme is responsive care. "Serve-and-return" describes responsive back-and-forth exchanges between child and caregiver, where the child's gaze, sounds, gestures, or needs are met by adult response. These interactions are described as important for the development of brain architecture and as a foundation for later social, linguistic, and cognitive abilities (Harvard Center on the Developing Child).

Recent scoping reviews describe responsive caregiving as a key component of nurturing care and as important for early development across caregiver, child, cultural, and socioeconomic contexts (Lobo et al., 2025). There is therefore a strong tendency in the field: it is not simply the quantity of stimulation, but the quality, timing, and contingency of the response that matters.

### How PABEN Relates to the Theme

If PABEN is assumed, this is exactly what one would expect. Responsive care matters because it makes the child's own signals effective in the world. The child cries, looks, babbles, reaches out, or tenses the body. If the world, especially the caregiver, responds stably and in time, the child's actions become coupled to continuation and relief.

Responsive care therefore does not only build relation. It builds the child's basic expectation that its own open skills can find continuation in the world. This corresponds to the design principle that learning requires actual coactivation, and that connections are formed only between elements that are active together in a functional now.

## 3. Contingency Learning and Early Agency

### What the Field Says

Contingency learning concerns how infants learn connections between their own actions and subsequent events. Recent reviews emphasize that a central open question is whether infants demonstrate actual agency in contingency learning, and that the field is moving from simple paradigms toward broader understandings of infant action, learning, and regulation (see Lobo et al., 2025; Wass et al., 2024).

Contingency research matters because it places the child's own activity at the center. The child is not only a receiver of input; the child tests, repeats, adjusts, and discovers relations between action and effect.

### How PABEN Relates to the Theme

PABEN would see contingency learning as one of the most fundamental forms of cognitive development. The child learns that a skill in a context can lead to a new context. The important point is not that the child already has a reflective intention. The important point is that action and consequence become bound through repeated experienced co-occurrence.

From a PABEN perspective, early agency is not necessarily "I decide this" in the adult sense. It is rather: my activity makes a difference in continuation. Agency does not begin as self-conscious will, but as stabilized perception-action continuations.

## 4. Still-Face and Social Expectation

### What the Field Says

The still-face paradigm is a classical tool for studying infants' social expectations. When an interacting caregiver suddenly becomes expressionless and non-responsive, infants typically show reduced positive affect, less gaze toward the caregiver, and increased negative affect (Mesman, van IJzendoorn & Bakermans-Kranenburg).

Newer work also problematizes how "still" the still-face actually is, and suggests that parent-infant interaction during still-face may be more persistent and relationally complex than the classical manipulation suggests (Ahn & Martin, 2024). This points to an important modern nuance: the infant is not merely reacting to the face as a stimulus, but to a break in an expected interaction.

### How PABEN Relates to the Theme

PABEN would understand still-face as a break in social continuation. The child has an open expectation field: face, gaze, rhythm, voice, and response usually carry interaction forward. When the response stops, continuation temporarily collapses.

The central issue is therefore not simply "less stimulation", but that the social context no longer carries the Belief it normally supports. Still-face becomes a very clean illustration of the difference between presence and responsive continuation.

## 5. Co-Regulation and the Transition to Self-Regulation

### What the Field Says

Another central theme is the transition from co-regulation to self-regulation. Recent reviews describe development as a movement from regulatory processes carried between child and caregiver toward regulatory processes that the child can increasingly carry alone (Wass et al., 2024). Research on infant emotion regulation also emphasizes individual differences and how caregiver behavior contributes to the development of emotion regulation both directly and in interaction with the child's own characteristics.

### How PABEN Relates to the Theme

PABEN can formulate this transition very tightly: self-regulation is internalized continuation. The child starts with regulation through external contexts, especially the caregiver. The caregiver makes the child's own continuations more stable. Over time, repeated regulatory sequences can become internal B/E structures, so the child can gradually carry more of the regulation itself.

This also clarifies an important developmental point: social animals do not begin life with fully independent personal Beliefs. Early life is carried by shared and caregiver-supported belief-fields. "Own Beliefs" gradually emerge through differentiation, internalization, and stabilization of routes that were first carried in the caregiver field.

Co-regulation does not require the child and caregiver to "merge." The individual remains an individual. External contexts can carry Beliefs for the individual, but they do not become part of the individual's system.

## 6. Attachment and Secure Base

### What the Field Says

Secure base is a core concept in attachment theory. Research describes the caregiver as a secure base from which the child can explore the world and to which the child can return during distress. Recent work also applies the secure-base concept in institutional and professional care contexts, for example in daycare and other care environments (Ye et al., 2024).

### How PABEN Relates to the Theme

PABEN would not explain secure base as a separate cognitive mechanism. It would understand secure base as the effect of high X in a particular context. The caregiver becomes the context in which the child's Beliefs work best: signals are heard, distress is regulated, needs are met, surprise is repaired, exploration has fallback, and the world becomes more manageable.

Secure base is therefore the context in which the child's available Beliefs have the greatest coverage reliability. It also widens the child's Belief-horizon: because fallback is available, more possible routes can be explored without the whole field becoming threatening. Safety is not only emotional warmth; it is increased continuation capacity and a broader reachable future.

## 7. Attachment to Objects and Imaginary Objects

### What the Field Says

Developmental research and clinical developmental theory have long been interested in children's attachment to special objects. Winnicott's classical concept of transitional objects described the child's first "not-me possession": an object that is neither the child itself nor merely a neutral external thing, but which can function in the transition between full dependence on the caregiver and more independent regulation (Winnicott, 1953).

Recent reviews of object attachment similarly describe how attachment to objects can support emotion regulation by both up-regulating positive affect and down-regulating negative affect (Timpano et al., 2021). A recent study of transitional attachment objects and stress response also found that physical interaction with an attachment object can affect physiological stress measures during recovery, even when self-reported emotion regulation does not necessarily change in the same way (Ko & Liang, 2024).

In parallel, research on imaginary companions shows that imaginary friends and other imaginary figures are not merely random fantasy. Studies have linked imaginary companions to social-cognitive and emotional developmental areas such as theory of mind, emotion understanding, coping, and social practice (Gimenez-Dasi et al., 2016; Gleason, 2017). The field therefore suggests that children do not attach regulation only to people, but also to things, rituals, places, stories, and imaginary relations.

### How PABEN Relates to the Theme

This theme expands the secure-base concept. If PABEN is assumed, attachment is not only binding to persons, but binding to high-X contexts. The caregiver is one type of high-X context, but an object, ritual, place, or imaginary figure can also become attachment-bearing if it is repeatedly coactive with regulation, relief, sleep, safety, caregiver proximity, or stable continuation.

A transitional object is therefore secure base in portable form. The object does not contain care in itself, but it reactivates the continuations in which care was previously stabilized. It can also extend the Belief-horizon outside the caregiver's immediate presence, because the child can carry a familiar route into sleep, separation, waiting, or distress. Imaginary objects can similarly be understood as internally simulated high-X contexts where the child can practice social, emotional, and action-related continuations without full external dependency. This makes object and fantasy attachment central in the transition from co-regulation to self-regulation.

## 8. Embodied Cognition and Perception-Action

### What the Field Says

Embodied cognition has received renewed attention, also in relation to artificial intelligence and development. The field emphasizes that thinking is grounded in body, movement, and environment (Barrett et al., 2024). In developmental psychology, embodied cognition has long emphasized that children's conceptual and language development depends on bodily experiences and action in the world (Wellsby & Pexman, 2014).

### How PABEN Relates to the Theme

PABEN fits well with an embodied developmental understanding because the model does not begin with abstract symbols. It begins with context, skill, and need. The child does not first learn "about the world" and act afterward. The child learns the world through possible continuations: what can this context carry, which skill works here, what happens afterward, and does it cover something?

Development is bodily from the beginning, not as a supplement to cognition, but as cognition's operative form.

## 9. Play, Exploration, and Learning

### What the Field Says

Play is broadly emphasized as central to children's cognitive, social, emotional, and motor development. Reviews of play in children's development describe an extensive research base linking play with learning and development across several areas (Whitebread et al., 2017). Recent studies of object exploration also emphasize that children's exploration of objects is considered a driver of motor, cognitive, and social development, and that physical and social environments shape the possibilities for exploration (van Liempd et al., 2024).

### How PABEN Relates to the Theme

PABEN should not understand play as a separate process or a special play module. Play is rather a general term for Beliefs and actions run inside a consequence-weakened frame. This can include physical play, social play, games, fantasy, inner mental play, or rule invention. The ordinary processes remain active: Try-Observe, Reflection, and Recognize-Execute can all occur in play.

The difference is that consequence is placed in brackets, allowing the system to test skills, roles, rules, and possible worlds without full reality binding. Play is therefore surplus-opened rather than directly Need-driven: when basic pressures are sufficiently covered, capacity is released for freer C/S unfolding. This is also why play can widen the Belief-horizon: the child can simulate and test routes that are not yet safe, needed, or possible in ordinary execution.

But play is not N-free. The child will still avoid strong negative N pressure: pain, danger, shame, exclusion, defeat, or collapse of the play field. When something becomes at stake, such as winning, belonging, mastering, protecting the rule, or avoiding injury, N pressure can quickly be recruited, and play can shift toward work, conflict, or regulation. The PABEN reading is therefore: lack binds, coverage calms, surplus plays, but stakes can pull play back into stabilization work.

## 10. Preliminary Overall Assessment of the Research Landscape

Across recent developmental research, several themes recur:

- The child is active: it learns through action, signals, exploration, and contingency.
- Development is relational: caregivers are not merely an external environment, but regulatory partners.
- Timing matters: responsiveness, synchrony, and contingency are decisive.
- Self-regulation develops from co-regulation: the child first carries regulation through others, and later increasingly alone.
- Social breaks are developmentally powerful: still-face shows that breaks in social response affect the child's expectation system.
- Safety enables exploration: secure base and play are closely linked because stable coverage opens consequence-weakened frames for freer testing and gradually widens the Belief-horizon.
- Early deprivation affects broad developmental trajectories, especially when care is non-responsive, unstable, or institutionally poor.
- Cognition is bodily and situated: perception, action, and environment are not secondary inputs to cognition, but part of cognition's developmental form.
- Attachment can be carried by several context types: people, objects, places, rituals, stories, and imaginary objects can all function as regulation-bearing high-X contexts.

**PABEN comment:** PABEN positions itself as one possible mechanical synthesis of these themes. It does not say that developmental research proves PABEN. It says that if PABEN is assumed, these developmental phenomena appear as expected effects of one mechanism: actuality, coactivation, binding, X stabilization, E closure, Belief-horizon expansion, and gradual internalization of continuation.
