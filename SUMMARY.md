# PABEN at a Glance

The work on PABEN has gradually brought a set of interesting relations into view. Perception, action, belief, experience, need, emotion, and consciousness appear less like separate faculties and more like different aspects of one continuity-regulating architecture.

At the center of this architecture is the problem of maintaining usable continuity between what is present, what can be done, what is expected, what has been experienced, and what must be fulfilled.

PABEN separates this continuity into five domains: **Perception**, **Action**, **Belief**, **Experience**, and **Need**. These are not isolated modules, but different sides of the same regulatory structure. Perception recognizes the current world as contexts. Action carries skills that can transform or maintain those contexts. Beliefs bind context and skill into usable promises: *do this while this is true*. Experience remembers lived transitions between contexts. Need supplies the pressure that makes some transitions matter.

In this view, a belief is not merely an idea. It is an executable expectation. It says that a certain skill, used in a certain context, can move the organism toward a more stable or more fulfilled state. This is why belief becomes the central unit of the model. The organism does not act directly on the world as raw matter. It acts through beliefs about which skills can be used in which contexts, and what those uses are expected to change.

The quality of such a belief is expressed as **Expectancy**:

$$
X(B) = Q \cdot T \cdot R \cdot V
$$

Here, **Q** is the executability of the skill, **T** is the trustability of the context, **R** is the reachability of the experienced path, and **V** is the valencability of the need-relief.

| Dimension             | Meaning                 | Stability Question                      |
| --------------------- | ----------------------- | --------------------------------------- |
| **Q / Executability** | Skill stability         | Can the skill be carried out reliably?  |
| **T / Trustability**  | Context stability       | Can the context be trusted as usable?   |
| **R / Reachability**  | Access and continuation | Is there a reachable path from here?    |
| **V / Valencability** | Fulfillment access      | Will this actually matter for the need? |

A belief is strong when the organism can execute the skill, trust the context, reach the remembered route, and expect the result to matter. A belief weakens when one of these dimensions becomes uncertain, blocked, disturbed, or broken.

This gives the model its basic engine. The organism opens beliefs when something must be stabilized or fulfilled. It acts through the best available belief. It observes whether the world remains within expectation. If the belief succeeds, continuity is strengthened. If it fails, the organism must repair, revise, search, or learn.

The three major processes describe how this happens.

**Recognize–Execute** is the ordinary flow of life. The organism recognizes a context and executes a usable skill inside it. Most of this can run without reflection.

**Try–Observe** is the exploratory process. The organism varies action and observation in order to discover what works, refine skills, and distinguish usable context variants.

**Reflection** is the repair and construction process. When the current belief-field cannot carry continuity, reflection searches for a new hook: a better context, a better skill, or a new belief that can restore viability.

Consciousness, in this framework, is not placed outside the mechanism as a separate substance. It is the occupied part of the belief-field where stabilization is most urgent. **Attention** selects the belief with the strongest unresolved change. **Occupation** is drawn toward the domain where the unresolved change is greatest. Consciousness is therefore not everywhere at once; it is where the system most needs to restore or secure continuity.

This is where emotion becomes central.

Emotion is the felt form of change in belief-expectancy. It is not an ornament added after cognition. It is the organism’s registration of whether a belief is opening, improving, failing, resolving, or collapsing. Formally, emotion is tied to:

$$
dX(B)
$$

The sign of this change gives valence. Its size gives amplitude. Its location gives the occupied dimension. Its phase gives the emotional kind.

This leads to one of the model’s most important findings: the basic emotions can be derived from the belief-cycle itself.

| Belief Phase | Positive / Appetitive      | Negative / Aversive |
| ------------ | -------------------------- | ------------------- |
| Surprise     | Appetitive surprise        | Aversive surprise   |
| Potential    | Desire / Lyst              | Fear / Frygt        |
| Realised     | Joy / Glæde                | Sorrow / Sorg       |
| Failed       | Disappointment / Skuffelse | Relief / Lettelse   |

The table is important because it suggests that emotions are not arbitrary labels. They emerge from the same regulatory structure that selects beliefs and guides action. A desired future is an open positive belief. Fear is an open negative belief. Joy is a successful positive closure. Sorrow is a realized negative outcome. Disappointment is a failed positive expectation. Relief is a failed negative expectation.

In other words, emotion is the organism feeling the fate of its own beliefs.

The same structure can then be extended to social life.

A social animal does not only regulate its own belief-field. It also lives inside shared belief-fields. Others are not just external objects. They are providers, witnesses, threats, helpers, judges, partners, and carriers of possible contexts. In early life this is even more fundamental: the organism begins in dependency on caregiver belief-fields before its own beliefs are fully differentiated and stabilized.

Social emotion therefore begins when expectancy changes inside a belief-field that is shared with, dependent on, or judged by another agent or group. The question is no longer only: *Can I do this while this is true?* The question becomes: *Can I do this here, among these others, inside this shared belief-field?*

This means that social emotions are not separate from the basic emotion mechanism. They are overlays on it. The basic layer still registers change in expectancy. The social layer specifies that the change occurs in a dependency relation between self, other, group, action, context, access, or fulfillment.

The same four expectancy dimensions apply socially.

| Social Dimension      | Shared Dependency                   | Core Question                                                     |
| --------------------- | ----------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Q / Executability** | Skill and action contribution       | Who acts, helps, fails, or carries responsibility?                |
| **T / Trustability**  | Context preservation or disturbance | Who protects, disturbs, violates, or restores the shared context? |
| **R / Reachability**  | Access, permission, and recognition | Who is allowed, denied, recognized, or excluded?                  |
| **V / Valencability** | Fulfillment, attachment, and care   | Who lacks, receives, gives, or withholds fulfillment?             |

A social emotion appears only where the agent depends on the relevant shared dimension. If another agent’s action does not matter for my skill, context, access, or fulfillment, then it may be observed, but it does not become socially emotional. It becomes socially emotional when their action changes the stability of a belief-field I depend on.

Parentheses indicate the coupled or reciprocal position in the same dependency relation. Gratitude implies that someone else carried a duty. Respect implies that someone else displayed worthiness. Shame implies that others may hold anger. Humiliation implies that others may gloat. Love and compassion are likewise coupled through fulfillment-dependency: one side lacks, receives, or attaches; the other side cares, responds, or fails to respond.

The important point is not that every word in the table is final. The important point is the structure. Social emotions fall naturally into place when the basic emotion mechanism is applied to shared belief-dependencies. They arise when the stability of action, context, access, or fulfillment depends on another agent or group.

This also explains why social systems develop repair mechanisms. Apology, penance, punishment, forgiveness, exclusion, recognition, care, gratitude, respect, and reconciliation are not cultural ornaments placed on top of emotion. They are stabilizers of shared expectancy. They determine whether a damaged belief-relation can be restored, whether the agent can re-enter the shared field, or whether exclusion must protect the field from further deformation.

The larger picture is therefore:

A living system must keep itself continuous.
It does so through beliefs.
Beliefs bind context, skill, experience, and need.
Expectancy measures how viable a belief is.
Emotion is the felt change in that viability.
Social emotion is the felt change in shared belief-dependency.
Consciousness is drawn to the belief where stabilization matters most.

The striking result is that emotion and social emotion do not need to be introduced as separate mysteries. They fall out naturally as regulators of belief-continuity: first within the individual organism, then within the shared fields that social organisms depend on.

This is also why PABEN appears to have surprisingly strong explanatory power. It remains speculative, and it is still immature as an implementation. But as a conceptual architecture, it begins to connect perception, action, belief, experience, need, emotion, attention, consciousness, and social regulation in one coherent picture. Its promise is not that every detail is already in place. Its promise is that the same mechanism keeps reappearing where separate explanations are usually required.
