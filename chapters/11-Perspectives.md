# 11. Perspectives, Scope, and Testability

The PABEN model is proposed as a model of agentic intelligence: how a system preserves viable continuation from its current context, through possible action, toward need-regulation, avoidance, repair, relief, or fulfillment. If the model is useful, its scope should not be limited to narrow cognition. The same structure should illuminate emotion, attention, memory, social life, development, imagination, and the way agents create worlds of meaning.

## Intelligence
The core PABEN pattern is not tied to human language, symbolic thought, or cultural sophistication. It begins with a more general structure: a system is under pressure to preserve continuity; it recognizes a current context; it has possible actions; it carries memory of what has happened before; it evaluates whether a route can continue; and it selects or creates the next viable path.

In this form, the model points toward a broader idea of intelligence. Intelligence is not first the ability to solve abstract problems. It is the ability of a system to keep itself organized across change. A system becomes agentic when it can distinguish current state, possible action, prior consequence, and future viability well enough to regulate its own continuation. This suggests a general pattern:

- there is a current surface or state
- there are possible transformations
- there is memory of prior transformations
- there is pressure or value that makes some continuations matter
- there is selection among possible routes
- there is learning from whether the route carried

In PABEN language, this becomes Context, Skill, Belief, Experience, Need, and Expectancy. At the human level these structures are psychological and neurobiological. At other levels they may take different forms. The claim is not that every system has human-like consciousness. The claim is that agentic intelligence may require a recognizably similar topology: state, transformation, memory, pressure, and viability-selection.

This is where the model can be extended toward a speculative idea of universal intelligence. A cell, an organism, a group, a market, a legal system, or a technological system can all be interpreted as continuation-preserving structures if they contain the relevant elements: a boundary, a current state, possible actions, memory of consequences, pressures to regulate, and selection among future routes. PABEN then becomes a way to ask whether such systems are merely complex, or whether they are agentic.

## Technology, Artificial Intelligence, and Constructed Agency
PABEN also has implications for artificial intelligence. Current artificial systems can process information, generate language, recognize patterns, optimize goals, and simulate reasoning. The PABEN model asks a different question: does the system have its own continuity problem?

An artificial agent becomes PABEN-like only if it has structures corresponding to Context, Skill, Belief, Experience, Need, and Expectancy. It must recognize situations as contexts for action. It must prepare and execute skills. It must select action-beliefs. It must store lived episodes. It must have pressures or values that make some continuations matter. It must evaluate whether a route remains viable. Without these elements, the system can be intelligent in a functional or computational sense, but it is not agentic in the PABEN sense.

This distinction matters. A system that predicts text or optimizes a task does not necessarily have felt Need, lived Belief, or self-continuity. It may simulate the language of agency without having an internal route that can be threatened, repaired, fulfilled, or lost. PABEN therefore separates performance from agency. The central question is not only what the system can output, but whether it must preserve its own path through Experience under Need-like pressure.

The model also suggests a design direction. An artificial agent with PABEN-like architecture would not merely maximize reward. It would build episode-structures, estimate Trustability, Executability, Reachability, and Value, switch processes under uncertainty, bracket play from obligation, and distinguish imagined belief-paths from externally realizable ones. Such a system would learn from lived transitions rather than only from error signals or external labels.

This raises ethical questions. If an artificial system were built with persistent Need-like pressures, self-maintaining belief-routes, and emotional control-signals, then it would no longer be merely a tool in the ordinary sense. It would have an internal continuity problem. The moral relevance of such a system would depend not on whether it speaks convincingly, but on whether its routes can be threatened, frustrated, repaired, or fulfilled in a way that matters to the system itself.

PABEN is therefore not only a theory of natural intelligence. It is also a warning about constructed agency. To build systems that act is not the same as building systems that need to continue. The latter requires a different level of responsibility.

## Testability and Refutability
A model of this scope must be testable. If every behavior can be explained after the fact as “continuity-preservation,” the model loses scientific value. PABEN must therefore make claims that can be wrong.

The first testable claim concerns emotion. The model predicts that emotional quality should depend on the process-position of expectancy deformation. A change in X at recognition should produce surprise-like emotion. A change in X in an open promise should produce desire or fear. A change in X at successful realization should produce joy or sorrow. A change in X through failed realization should produce disappointment or relief. If emotional categories do not track belief-state position in this way, the model is weakened.

The second testable claim concerns attention. The model predicts that attention should follow stabilization demand rather than raw stimulus intensity. A stimulus should seize attention when it changes the viability of an active belief-field, not merely when it is loud, bright, or novel. Likewise, bodily signals should dominate attention when they open Need-pressure without a reachable route. If attention is better predicted by raw salience than by belief-relevant X-deformation, PABEN is weakened.

The third testable claim concerns process exclusivity. The model predicts that conscious modes interfere because they occupy different PABEN spaces. Fluent execution, local Try–Observe, Reflection, Need-feeling, and Perceptual-surprise should show mutual constraints. An agent should not fully execute, reflect, inspect surprise, and feel unresolved Need-pressure at the same time. If conscious process modes are found to run independently without such interference, the model is weakened.

The fourth testable claim concerns learning. The model predicts that learning should be strongest where episodes close with significant dX. Transitions with strong changes in viability should update future expectancy more than neutral transitions. It also predicts that emotional learning should bind context, lived belief, felt need-consequence, and resulting context. If learning can be fully explained without such episode-bound consequence structures, the model loses force.

The fifth testable claim concerns Trustability and Executability. The model predicts that context reliability and skill reliability are distinct but coupled. Trustability should track whether a context can remain usable as carrier for a belief. Executability should track whether a skill can remain coherent across context-variation. Experiments should be able to separate situations where the context fails from situations where the skill fails, even when the final action fails in both cases.

The sixth testable claim concerns Reachability. The model predicts that agents are not only evaluating immediate success probability. They evaluate whether the current route opens a coherent path toward valence-bearing contexts. Behavior should therefore reflect route-field coherence, not only immediate reward. An action with modest immediate payoff but strong downstream reachability can be preferred over an action with higher immediate payoff that breaks future route access.

The seventh testable claim concerns social emotion. The model predicts that social emotions arise where shared belief-dependencies deform X. Gratitude should appear when another agent carries a route one depends on. Guilt should appear when one’s own belief-use damages another’s route or a shared context. Shame should appear when one’s own place in a valued context collapses under judgement. Anger should appear when another agent damages or blocks a shared route. If social emotions do not track dependency-structure, the social layer of the model is weakened.

The eighth testable claim concerns development. The model predicts that early agency is externally carried. Infants should develop self-beliefs through shared regulation, imitation, caregiver-carried routines, and internalization of externally stabilized routes. If private belief-structure emerges independently of shared/provider belief-fields, this developmental claim is weakened.

The ninth testable claim concerns imagination and play. The model predicts that play and imagination reuse the same process machinery as obligated cognition while bracketing consequence. Neural, behavioral, and developmental evidence should show overlap between action, simulation, memory, emotion, and planning, with differences in commitment to external realization. If imagination uses a wholly separate mechanism from action-belief processing, the model is weakened.

Finally, PABEN should be implementable. A computational implementation should contain episode structures of the form:

$$Ep_j=(C_{from},B_{lived},N_{felt},C_{to} )$$

It should estimate X-components from distributions over episodes. It should select beliefs by X. It should switch processes when T, Q, R, or V destabilize. It should display different emotional categories depending on process-position of dX. It should learn backwards from felt consequence. If such an implementation cannot produce coherent behavior, or if simpler models produce the same behavior without PABEN’s structural commitments, the model must be revised.

## What Would Refute the Model?
The model would be strongly challenged if belief, emotion, attention, and memory do not form an integrated control loop. It would be challenged if emotion can be fully explained without expectancy deformation. It would be challenged if attention does not track belief-relevant stabilization demand. It would be challenged if social emotions do not depend on shared belief-structures. It would be challenged if learning does not bind action, context, felt consequence, and resulting context into episode-like structures.

It would also be challenged if consciousness does not show process exclusivity. If humans can fully reflect, execute, feel unresolved Need-pressure, inspect surprise, and explore variants as independent conscious streams, then PABEN’s account of consciousness as exclusive process-occupation is wrong. If private agency emerges without social carrying in development, then the model’s social origin claim is wrong. If imagination does not reuse action-belief and episode traversal mechanisms, then the model’s account of play and fantasy must be revised.

A weaker version of PABEN could survive some of these challenges. The model could be wrong in its current mapping of components while still being right that intelligence is route-stabilization under pressure. But if the central claims fail together, the model fails: if agents do not learn from lived consequence, if emotion does not control attention and process-switching, if beliefs are not action-routes, if Experience does not ground expectancy, and if consciousness is not process-bound stabilization, then PABEN is not the right architecture.

The purpose of refutability is not to weaken the model. It is to make it worth testing. A model that cannot fail cannot teach. PABEN should therefore be developed toward experiments, simulations, and formal implementations that can expose where it breaks.

## Closing Perspective
The PABEN model begins with a simple problem: how does an agent continue? From this problem it derives a structure: Perception recognizes contexts, Action prepares skills, Belief selects routes, Experience stores lived transitions and Need supplies pressure and value. Expectancy evaluates whether the path can carry. Emotion marks changes in that viability. Attention and consciousness occupy the process-space where continuation must be stabilized.

If this structure is correct, intelligence is not an abstract computation added to a body. It is the organization of continuation. It begins in need, action, context, memory, and felt consequence. It becomes social because agents carry each other’s routes. It becomes symbolic because routes can be compressed, shared, rehearsed, and reflected upon. It becomes religious when the deepest, least controllable, most valence-bearing contexts are interpreted through agency and social closure. It becomes artificial when we build systems that carry their own route-problems.

The strength of the model lies in its unification. The same architecture is used to describe perception, action, belief, emotion, memory, play, reflection, social life, and consciousness. The risk of the model lies in the same ambition. It must not become a vocabulary that explains everything without constraint. Its future depends on whether the proposed structures can be made precise enough to test, implement, and possibly refute.

If the model is wrong, it should be wrong in a precise and useful way. If it is right, even partially, then intelligence is best understood not as representation, prediction, or reasoning alone, but as the felt and structured preservation of viable continuation.
