# 5. Core Concepts and Definitions

This section defines the central concepts used in the PABEN model. The terms are used in a technical sense. The purpose is to stabilize the model vocabulary before the formal foundation is introduced.

## Agency Terms

### Agent

An agent is a system that must continue from its present situation toward regulation, relief, avoidance, repair, satisfaction, or fulfillment of its needs.

Agency requires a situated context, available skills, prior experience, active need-pressure or need-feedback, and an evaluative mechanism that selects viable continuations.

### Continuation

Continuation is the preservation of a possible path from where the agent is, through what it can do, toward what matters.

Continuation can be fluent, exploratory, or reflective. The agent continues forward through Recognize-Execute, sideways through Try-Observe, and upward through Reflection.

### Path

A path is a possible continuation from the present context through belief, skill, experience, and need-regulation.

A path is not only a sequence of events. It is a viability structure: a way for the agent to keep moving from the current situation toward a possible next state.

### Path Viability

Path viability is the degree to which a path can hold, be executed, continue, and matter.

Path viability is evaluated through Trustability, Executability, Reachability, and Valencability, and summarized by expectancy X.

## Domain Terms

### PABEN

PABEN is the five-domain architecture of the model: Perception, Action, Beliefs, Experience, and Needs.

Perception recognizes contexts. Action releases skills. Beliefs bind contexts and skills. Experience releases possible beliefs from learned transitions. Needs supply felt regulatory pressure and feedback.

### Perception / P

Perception is the domain of contexts.

It recognizes features, objects, relations, causes, roles, conditions, and situations. Perception identifies what kind of situation the agent is in and opens possible actions from that context.

Perception is distinct from felt need-states. The feeling of cold belongs to Needs. An open window, a cold surface, a draft, or low temperature belongs to Perception.

### Context / C

A context is a recognized situation, object, relation, cause, role, or condition that can matter for action.

Contexts are possibility-fields. They constrain, release, and stabilize possible skills and beliefs. They are hierarchical in nature, with lower-level contexts supporting higher-level context recognition.

### Context-Total

Context-total is the full set of learned context nodes available in Perception.

It is the agent's total context repertoire.

### Context-Recognized

Context-recognized is the set of contexts currently activated in Perception.

A context is recognized when perceptual input, persistent context structure, or internal activation is sufficient to activate that context node.

### Context-Surface

Context-surface is the top recognition-front of Perception.

It contains the highest currently recognized contexts that have not found an active parent in the present recognition process. Context-surface is what projects into Experience for opening a Belief-field.

Surprise occurs when something is recognized outside the currently carrying context-surface.

### Context-Carrier

Context-carrier is the context-vector that supports the currently intended belief.

It is not the full recognized context set. It is the part of the recognized context structure that carries the active continuity of the selected belief.

### Context-Required

Context-required is the context structure that must hold for a belief to become usable.

Context-required belongs to the static structure of a belief. Context-expected belongs to later live evaluation.

### Action / A

Action is the domain of skills.

It contains the executable patterns by which the agent can do something. Action includes bodily action, attention-shifting, speaking, social behavior, manipulation, searching, waiting, practicing, and other structured capacities the agent can release.

### Skill / S

A skill is an executable action-pattern.

A skill is what the agent can do when the relevant context is active. Skills become usable through their relation to contexts and beliefs. At the lowest level, a skill can bottom out in motor activation in the world.

### Skill-Total

Skill-total is the full set of learned skill nodes available in Action.

It is the agent's total skill repertoire.

### Skill-Candidates

Skill-candidates are skills that can become relevant under a recognized context, a belief, or a reflection process.

They are not yet released. They are possible skills that may become prepared or selected.

### Skill-Prepared

Skill-prepared is the skill structure placed in readiness by a Belief-intended.

Prepared does not mean executed. It means that the skill is ready for release if the belief continues to hold.

### Skill-Released

Skill-released is the skill structure released into action.

The model keeps intention in the belief domain. Skills are prepared and released; beliefs are intended.

### Needs / N

Needs are the domain of felt regulatory pressure and feedback.

Needs make continuation matter by supplying bodily and dependency-based pressure and by registering felt need-consequences. Needs do not store threats, promises, meanings, opportunities, failures, or dangers as such. Those are belief- and experience-mediated evaluations of what a felt need-state means in a context.

### Need / N_i

A Need is a specific regulatory profile within the Needs domain.

A Need can become active as pressure, and it can be registered as present felt feedback. The same Need can therefore appear as both N-pressure and N-felt at different points in the cycle.

### N-Pressure

N-pressure is active need-pressure that demands regulation, relief, preservation, repair, or continuation.

N-pressure is not yet a belief about the world. It is the felt regulatory push that makes the agent search for, preserve, or repair a viable path.

### N-Felt

N-felt is the present felt need-state or need-consequence.

N-felt is what the Needs domain registers now: for example pain, hunger, cold, fatigue, tension, warmth, satiety, relief, calm, contact-comfort, or contact-distress. N-felt can be positive, negative, or mixed in valence.

N-felt is not identical to emotion. Emotion arises when N-pressure or N-felt is bound through Experience and evaluated through belief-expectancy. Pain is N-felt. "This is dangerous" is an evaluation. Fear is an emotion carried by an open belief about possible negative continuation.

### N-Active

N-active is a shorthand for the currently relevant Need state:

$$
N_{active}(t)=N_{pressure}(t)\cup N_{felt}(t)
$$

N-active can be used when a formula only needs to say that Experience is influenced by currently active need-pressure and/or currently felt need-consequence.

## Binding Terms

### Belief / B

A belief is an action-belief.

It is a functional expectation that a skill can be applied in a context. In its simplest form, a belief says: while in this context, this skill can be used.

A belief binds Perception and Action together. It is the agent's usable action-claim about how continuation can proceed from a recognized situation.

### Belief-Total

Belief-total is the full set of learned beliefs.

It is the agent's total repertoire of context-skill action-claims.

### Belief-Field

A Belief-field is the current set of beliefs that can be selected now.

The Belief-field is released from Experience based on Context-surface and N-active. It contains the agent's presently available ways forward.

Some beliefs are strong and ready for execution. Others are weak, uncertain, blocked, risky, latent, or only partly available. The selected belief becomes attended, intended, and possibly released into action.

### Belief-Intended

Belief-intended is the belief selected from the current Belief-field.

Belief-intended carries intention and continuity. It prepares skill structure and is supported by a context-carrier.

### Belief-Horizon

The Belief-horizon is the highest level of abstraction at which the agent can keep a Belief-field stable as a viable expectation toward future valence.

It is not merely how far ahead the agent can think in time. It is how abstract a continuation the agent can hold stable while lower-level contexts, skills, and sub-beliefs change beneath it.

The Belief-field contains what can be selected now. The Belief-horizon contains higher-level routes the agent can hold as possible ways forward.

### Belief-Blanket

A Belief-blanket is a wider route-field around a problem, need-state, or horizon.

It is a set of overlapping beliefs and experience routes that provide possible continuation coverage. Reflection searches for hooks into a Belief-field or Belief-blanket.

### Experience / E

Experience is the domain of learned transitions through realized episodes.

Experience records what happened when beliefs were lived or used. It binds the starting context, the lived belief, the felt need-consequence, the resulting context, and the valence of the transition.

Experience releases possible beliefs into the current Belief-field and provides the route-structure held in the Belief-horizon.

### Episode

An episode is an experienced transition from a starting context, through a lived belief with a felt need-consequence, to a resulting context.

In compact form:

$$
C_{from} \xrightarrow{B_{lived}(N_{felt})} C_{to}
$$

Episodes are the material from which Experience is built. Repeated episodes strengthen belief-connections, context-skill relations, and expected continuations.

### B-Lived

B-lived is the belief as actually carried through an episode.

It is not merely the belief as stored structure. It is the belief as lived in a specific transition, with an actual N-felt consequence.

### Hook

A hook is a connection from a current unstable, unresolved, or new situation into a known or possible valence-bearing Belief-field, Belief-blanket, or route.

A new belief becomes meaningful when it hooks into a known field of regulation, relief, avoidance, repair, satisfaction, fulfillment, or continuation.

## Evaluation Terms

### Expectancy / X

Expectancy, abbreviated X, is the viability of a possible path.

X describes whether a belief-path can hold as a continuation from the present context toward need-regulation, relief, avoidance, repair, satisfaction, or fulfillment.

A belief has high X when the required context is usable, the skill can be executed, the path can continue, and the path preserves access to what matters.

A belief has low X when the required context, skill, continuation, or valencability is unstable, blocked, uncertain, or lost.

### Trustability / T

Trustability is the precision of context-usability relative to a belief.

T concerns whether the required context can carry the belief with low required correction, attention, or action-variation. High T means that little skill-variance is needed to make and keep the context usable as belief-carrier. Low T means that high skill-variation, correction, tracking, or attention is needed.

### Executability / Q

Executability is the precision of skill-execution relative to context variation.

Q concerns whether the agent can release and carry out the required skill across the relevant variations of the context. High Q means that the skill can be executed with low context-driven variation. Low Q means that many variants, corrections, or tracking operations are needed.

### Reachability / R

Reachability is the continuation-strength of the current path.

R concerns whether there are viable next steps from the present context through Experience. It describes whether the continuation-field remains open, connected, and navigable.

### Valencability / V

Valencability is the degree to which a belief-path preserves, restores, approaches, avoids, or transforms access to relevant valence.

V is why the path matters. T, Q, and R describe whether the path can hold, execute, and continue. V describes whether the path remains connected to positive or negative need-consequences through Experience.

### Valence

Valence is the felt positive or negative significance of N-felt relative to the agent's needs.

Valence does not by itself define a threat, promise, loss, or opportunity. Those require belief- and experience-mediated evaluation. Valence supplies the positive, negative, or mixed felt direction that makes an outcome matter.

### Valence-Bearing

Something is valence-bearing when it can carry expected or remembered need-consequence.

A context, belief, path, episode, social relation, or object can become valence-bearing because Experience has connected it to N-felt outcomes such as pain, relief, hunger, satiety, tension, calm, contact-distress, or contact-comfort.

### Relief

Relief is a positive N-felt produced by reduction, removal, avoidance, or release of aversive N-pressure.

Relief belongs to the Need side as felt regulatory feedback, but the meaning of relief depends on the belief-path and experience through which it occurs.

### Fulfillment

Fulfillment is a positive or sufficient N-felt in which an appetitive or promissory need-path reaches satisfaction, completion, stabilization, or restored access.

Fulfillment is not the general name for all N-felt. Pain, cold, fatigue, or contact-distress are N-felt, but they are not fulfillment. Fulfillment names a positive or sufficient form of need-regulation.

## Process Terms

### Recognize-Execute

Recognize-Execute is the fluent process mode.

A context is recognized, Experience releases a Belief-field, one belief becomes dominant, and a skill is released. The agent continues without reconstructing each step because Perception, Belief, and Action are already coordinated.

Recognize-Execute moves forward through an already viable path.

### Try-Observe

Try-Observe is the exploratory learning mode.

The agent holds a context in attention and varies skills to observe what changes, what remains stable, what the context can bear, and which variants belong together.

Try-Observe drives play, practice, curiosity, and local context-skill learning. It moves sideways within the local situation by testing nearby variants.

### Reflection

Reflection is the process mode that searches for hooks and reconstructs possible continuation when the current Belief-field lacks a sufficiently viable path.

Reflection works on the Belief-horizon. It connects the present situation to a known or possible valence-bearing route.

Reflection does not create meaning from nothing. It searches for a route back into meaningful continuation. It moves upward toward a higher-level belief that can hold the situation together.

### Surprise

Surprise occurs when something is recognized outside the currently carrying context-surface.

Surprise is not merely a mismatch inside an open belief. It is the appearance of something that does not belong to the active context-field and therefore requires evaluation.

### Emotion

Emotion is the felt direction and amplitude of a change in expectancy.

When X changes, the agent feels that a path has become more or less possible, promising, dangerous, stable, blocked, realized, or lost. These are not stored directly in Needs; they arise when N-pressure or N-felt is bound through Experience and evaluated through Belief-expectancy.

Emotion guides attention, learning, and process switching.

### Attention

Attention is the pointer to one belief.

Attention points to the current problem-belief: the belief whose unresolved expectancy, instability, promise, threat, or consequence matters most.

Attention does not solve the problem. It marks which belief the system must work on.

### Consciousness

Consciousness is the active stabilization process around the attended belief.

Depending on what the attended belief requires, consciousness can occupy Perception, Action, Belief, Experience, or Need-related stabilization.

Where attention points, consciousness works.

### Learning

Learning is the establishment or strengthening of structure through attention, priming, co-activation, simultaneity, emotion, and consequence.

Learning is constructive rather than editorial. The agent builds new connections, hooks, and pathways. The model does not rely on a central mechanism that rewrites the whole structure.

### Valence-Backward Learning

Valence-backward learning is the principle that the model learns meaning from valence backwards.

The agent discovers places where N-felt becomes significant, and then builds context-skill-belief structure backward from those valenced outcomes.

### Priming

Priming is the activation of related contexts, skills, beliefs, experiences, or needs so they become available for selection or learning.

Priming prepares possible continuations before one of them is selected or released.

### Co-Activation

Co-activation is the simultaneous activation of elements across domains, such as a context, a skill, a belief, a need-state, and an outcome.

Co-activation establishes and strengthens relations in the model.

### Zip / Context-Skill Zip

The zip is the coordinated unfolding of context and skill during action.

As the agent acts, contexts release skills and skills lead into new contexts. In learned action, this unfolds fluently without conscious reconstruction at each step.

## Social Terms

### Social Context

A social context is a context in which beliefs, actions, and outcomes are evaluated in relation to other agents and shared provider-contexts.

Social contexts contain fields of permission, recognition, responsibility, care, repair, status, and belonging.

### Social Emotion

A social emotion is an emotion arising from changes in expectancy within a shared or socially judged context.

Social emotions concern how the agent's or another agent's beliefs and actions align or misalign with the shared context and its expected valence.
