# 1. Preface

The PABEN model presented here is an independent theoretical attempt to describe cognition, agency, emotion, attention, and consciousness as parts of one integrated functional architecture.

It should be read as a speculative model in the proper sense of the word: not as an established theory, and not as a summary of the current scientific consensus, but as a structured proposal for how intelligent behavior might be mechanized from a small number of interacting principles. The model is offered with full respect for the depth and difficulty of neuroscience, cognitive science, psychology, artificial intelligence, and philosophy of mind. Many of the questions addressed here have long histories, and no simple framework should pretend to settle them.

At the same time, the model is not intended as a loose metaphor. It has been developed over many years as a serious attempt to “work out” the necessary architecture of an agent: what must be in place for perception to guide action, for experience to shape expectation, for needs to generate direction, for emotion to regulate attention, and for consciousness to occupy the point of greatest unresolved consequence.

The approach is deliberately constructive and engineering-oriented. Rather than beginning from the full complexity of the brain or from existing theoretical vocabularies, the model begins from a simpler question: what is the minimum functional structure an agent would need in order to act intelligently, learn from consequence, preserve access to fulfillment, and adapt its behavior over time?

This also means that the model avoids, where possible, routes that have historically led to explanatory dead ends: detached representation without action, cognition without valence, emotion without function, attention without need, and intelligence without embodiment. The aim is not to reject existing research, but to propose a different organizing frame — one that tries to keep perception, action, belief, experience, and need mechanically connected from the start.

The result is necessarily provisional. The concepts, formal relations, and process descriptions should be treated as hypotheses: tools for thinking, testing, refining, and possibly discarding. But the ambition is serious. If the model is wrong, it should be wrong in a precise and useful way. If it is partly right, it may offer a compact way to understand how agency, expectation, emotion, and consciousness belong to the same underlying problem.
