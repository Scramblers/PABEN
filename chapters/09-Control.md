# 9. Attention, Process Control, and Consciousness

The process layer described how the agent can stabilize continuation by moving forward, sideways, or upward through the PABEN structure. This chapter describes how the agent selects between those processes, how attention is directed, and why consciousness must occupy one functional space at a time. The model treats consciousness not as a separate substance, but as the active occupation of a process-domain under the demand of continuity. Where attention points, consciousness works.

The agent is not conscious of everything it contains. It cannot occupy the whole PABEN structure at once. Perception can recognize many contexts, Action can prepare many skills, Experience can release many belief-fields, Belief can compare possible routes, and Needs can apply many pressures. But conscious work is exclusive. At any moment, consciousness is committed to one stabilizing task: living a belief forward, stabilizing variants sideways, reflecting upward, feeling Need-pressure, or resolving perceptual disturbance. The agent can switch quickly between these modes, but it cannot fully be in them all at the same time.

This exclusivity follows from the function of consciousness. Consciousness exists where the agent must preserve continuity under uncertainty. It is not needed for fully stabilized execution. It is needed where the path is open, unstable, threatened, tempting, blocked, painful, or not yet learned. Consciousness is therefore not the total content of the agent. It is the active process through which the agent tries to keep a belief-path viable.

Attention is the selection of the belief or disturbance that currently demands conscious work. It is not a spotlight cast on neutral representations. Attention is a commitment of process-capacity to the belief-path that most requires stabilization. When Need-pressure rises, attention is pulled toward the belief-routes that can regulate the felt state. When Perception reveals surprise, attention is pulled toward the context-surface that destabilizes the current route. When no immediate pressure dominates, attention can be released into play, curiosity, imagination, and exploration.

The central rule is simple: the agent must remain continuous. Continuity means that the agent must keep some path open from what it is living now to what can be lived next. Under pressure, this becomes obligation. Under surplus, it becomes play. The same PABEN processes are used in both cases. The difference is not the machinery, but the degree of consequence pressure attached to the process.

## Attention as Selection of Stabilization Demand
Attention is governed by changes in expectancy. A belief-path with high and stable X does not require conscious occupation. It can be carried by learned structure. A belief-path with falling, uncertain, ambiguous, or highly valuable X pulls attention because it threatens or promises a change in the agent’s continuity.

In compact terms, attention follows stabilization demand. A belief becomes attention-relevant when its current or expected change in X matters enough to require process-control. This includes negative pressure, such as fear, pain, loss, social threat, confusion, or blocked action. It also includes positive pressure, such as desire, curiosity, opportunity, play, attraction, and anticipated relief. Attention is not only drawn by problems. It is drawn by consequential openings.

The attention target is a belief or belief-related disturbance. A raw feature does not claim attention by itself. It claims attention when it changes the Context-surface in a way that opens, blocks, confirms, destabilizes, or competes with a belief-field. A bodily signal does not claim attention merely as sensation. It claims attention when it becomes Need-pressure and demands a route through Experience. Attention therefore always has a process meaning: something must be lived, stabilized, explored, repaired, avoided, or restructured.

The agent does not choose attention from outside itself. Attention is the internal priority result of the PABEN structure. Needs apply pressure. Perception changes the surface. Experience opens belief-fields. Beliefs compete by X. The selected belief or disturbance becomes the object of conscious work. What appears as “I attend to this” is, in the model, the moment where the agent’s continuity problem has selected a process-space.

Attention can be narrow or broad depending on X. When X is stable and several Needs remain covered, the agent can keep a broad horizon. Multiple Belief-blankets can remain open, and the agent can navigate while preserving many considerations at once. Under stress, rising emotional amplitude narrows Need-consideration. The system prioritizes the most urgent pressure and suppresses competing Needs. This produces focus, speed, and survival relevance, but it also reduces social nuance, long-range planning, curiosity, and play.

Attention is therefore both protective and costly. It protects continuity by giving process-capacity to what matters most now. It is costly because whatever is not attended can become under-maintained. Suppressed Needs, ignored contexts, and postponed repairs do not disappear. They remain as unresolved pressure in the structure and can return later as fatigue, guilt, shame, grief, rumination, craving, or renewed need.

## Process Control: Forward, Sideways, Upward, Inward, Outward
Process control selects how consciousness should work on the attended structure. The model distinguishes five basic process-directions: forward, sideways, upward, inward, and outward.

Forward control is Recognise–Execute. Consciousness occupies the current belief as something to be lived. The agent proceeds through the context-skill zip, allowing the intended belief to prepare skills, specify required contexts, and close retrospectively when a new belief takes over. Forward control is chosen when the route has enough X to be lived.

Sideways control is Try–Observe. Consciousness occupies the relation between context and skill as something to be stabilized. The agent holds one side and varies the other. This process is chosen when the path is not broken, but variance is too high for fluent execution. The agent must learn how the context holds, how the skill carries, what variants matter, and what can be controlled.

Upward control is Reflection. Consciousness occupies the belief-horizon. The agent cannot simply continue and cannot locally stabilize the current relation. It must find a hook: a higher-level context, a different skill, another route, a memory, a social support, a repair path, or a new interpretation. Reflection is chosen when the route-field loses coherence or when no local Belief-field carries sufficient X.

Inward control is Need-feeling. Consciousness occupies the felt pressure itself. The agent does not yet know what to do with the pressure, or the pressure must be registered before any route can be selected. Feeling is not a passive side-effect. It is the conscious occupation of Need-pressure as a demand for continuation. The felt state tells the agent that something in its viability must be regulated, relieved, endured, repaired, avoided, or fulfilled.

Outward control is Perceptual-surprise. Consciousness occupies the changed Context-surface. Something in the world-surface has entered, disappeared, shifted, contradicted, threatened, invited, or destabilized the current belief-field. The agent must inspect, confirm, reinterpret, track, or respond to the surface before it can continue. Surprise is not external input alone. It is the recognition that the current context no longer carries the belief-field as assumed.

These five directions define the basic attention grammar of the model. Consciousness can live the route, vary the route, restructure the route, feel the pressure, or inspect the surface. Every conscious mode is a way of preserving continuity under a specific kind of uncertainty.

## Consciousness as Exclusive Occupation of a PABEN Space
The PABEN domains are structurally connected, but consciousness cannot occupy them all with equal force at the same time. To consciously perceive is not the same as consciously execute. To consciously execute is not the same as consciously reflect. To consciously reflect is not the same as consciously feel. To consciously feel is not the same as consciously inspect the outer surface. Each mode requires a different occupation of the PABEN structure.

This exclusivity is not arbitrary. Each process requires a different kind of stability. Recognize–Execute requires the agent to stay with the living belief and allow the context-skill zip to carry. Try–Observe requires the agent to hold one side of a relation fixed while varying the other. Reflection requires the agent to suspend immediate execution and activate a wider belief-horizon. Need-feeling requires the agent to hold the felt pressure as the object. Perceptual-surprise requires the agent to hold the changed surface as the object.

If these processes were fully simultaneous, they would interfere with each other. Execution requires commitment. Reflection requires suspension of commitment. Try–Observe requires controlled variation. Fluent execution requires reduced variation. Feeling requires inward occupation. Perceptual inspection requires outward occupation. The processes can interleave rapidly, but each conscious moment has a dominant occupation.

This supports why attention feels like a limited resource. The limitation is not merely capacity in an abstract sense. It is structural exclusivity. The agent cannot fully live a belief, inspect its perceptual disruption, feel its bodily pressure, explore variants, and reflect on alternatives in the same conscious act. It can only switch among them.

The switching itself is guided by X. When forward execution remains viable, consciousness stays with the living belief or withdraws as execution becomes fluent. When context or skill variance rises, consciousness shifts sideways into Try–Observe. When Reachability collapses, consciousness shifts upward into Reflection. When Need-pressure becomes dominant, consciousness shifts inward into feeling. When the Context-surface changes in a belief-relevant way, consciousness shifts outward into surprise and inspection.

Consciousness is therefore not a theater in which all domains are displayed. It is the current worksite of the agent’s continuity problem. The content of consciousness is the domain currently required to keep the agent from losing its path.

## Obligation Under Need-Pressure
Need-pressure imposes obligation. When a Need becomes active, the agent is no longer free to treat all beliefs as equal possibilities. The system must find, maintain, or create a route to regulation, avoidance, relief, repair, or fulfillment. This is the basic meaning of concern in the model: a belief-path matters because a Need has made continuation dependent on it.

Under Need-pressure, Experience is opened. Prior episodes are searched or activated because they contain the history of what has previously carried similar felt consequences. The agent does not need a direct Need-to-Context connection. The route is mediated by episodes. Contexts become relevant because they have carried beliefs that were lived with felt need-consequences. A Need activates Experience; Experience retrieves valence-bearing context-fields; Belief selects a route; Action attempts to live it; Perception confirms or disrupts the surface.

Obligation increases as X falls. If a route remains available, the agent can continue. If the route is unstable, it must stabilize. If the route is broken, it must reflect. If no route is found, the agent remains occupied by pressure. In this way the model can let unresolved Need dominate consciousness. The system cannot simply dismiss a Need that has no route. It must either find a belief-path, reduce the pressure, suppress the Need temporarily, or endure the unresolved state as mood, tension, or background distress.

Need-pressure also narrows the field of possible play. When the agent is under strong obligation, it cannot freely explore all variants. Exploration becomes instrumental: only variants relevant to regulation are tried. Reflection becomes urgent: only routes that promise relief, avoidance, repair, or fulfillment are prioritized. Perception becomes threat- or opportunity-sensitive. The agent’s world becomes smaller because continuity has become more difficult.

This narrowing is not a mistake. It is the natural consequence of endangered continuity. An agent that treated all possibilities equally under pain, danger, or social collapse would fail to preserve itself. Stress makes the agent less open because it must secure a route before openness can return. The cost is that suppressed Needs and ignored routes remain structurally unresolved.

## Play, Surplus, and Consequence-Bracketed Processing
Play occurs when the agent has surplus continuity. The active Needs are covered well enough that the agent can loosen immediate consequence pressure. This does not create a new machinery. Play uses the same processes as obligated cognition: Recognize–Execute, Try–Observe, Reflection, Need-feeling, and Perceptual inspection. The difference is that failure is bracketed.

In play, the agent can try beliefs without requiring immediate real-world fulfillment. It can explore context-skill variants without every error becoming a threat. It can reflect without needing an urgent answer. It can imagine actions, roles, dangers, rewards, and social situations without fully committing the body or the external world. Play is therefore not the opposite of cognition. It is cognition with loosened consequence-binding.

This supports why play is central to learning. Under surplus, the agent can widen T and Q without high cost. It can test how contexts hold, how skills carry, how routes might unfold, and what feelings could arise. It can build sibling contexts, skill variants, social scripts, counterfactual routes, and hypothetical beliefs. Many of these will never become real actions, but they enlarge the Belief-horizon.

Play also permits safe failure. A failed imagined route does not necessarily produce full bodily loss. A failed pretend action does not necessarily destroy the actual context. A failed social rehearsal does not necessarily damage the real relation. Because the cost is reduced, the agent can explore more widely. This produces structure that later becomes useful under obligation.

Play is therefore the surplus-form of continuity. When obligation is low, the agent does not stop being agentic. It continues to expand its possible continuations. It explores not because it lacks needs, but because its needs are sufficiently covered to allow route-expansion. Curiosity, practice, fantasy, humor, imitation, ritual, and pretend action all belong here: they are ways of using the same processes without requiring immediate realization.

## Imagination and Non-Realized Belief-Paths
Imagination is the use of PABEN processes without requiring immediate external realization. The agent can activate contexts, skills, beliefs, episodes, and felt tendencies internally, while bracketing the requirement that the belief be executable in the current outer surface. This makes imagination possible without adding a separate representational faculty. It is the same route-structure used under loosened reality-demand.

In imagination, a belief can become active without becoming fully Belief-intended for bodily execution. A skill can be prepared as simulated tendency rather than released movement. A context can be activated as an internal surface rather than externally recognized surface. Experience can traverse prior episodes and recombine them into possible routes. Need can supply felt relevance, fear, desire, longing, or relief without the external target being present.

This supports why imagined events can feel real enough to move the agent, but not real enough to require full action. The same structures are active, but their coupling to external execution is inhibited, bracketed, or weakened. The agent can live a belief internally as a possible path rather than externally as a committed path.

Fantasy is not false cognition in this model. It is route-processing without current realization demand. It allows the agent to explore “what would happen if” by letting belief-loops run through Experience, Perception-like imagery, Action-like simulation, and Need-like feeling. The output can be new expectations, fears, desires, plans, social rehearsals, or emotional closures.

The distinction between imagination and delusion is not that one uses a different machinery. The distinction is whether the agent preserves the boundary between internally traversed belief-paths and externally required belief-paths. In healthy imagination, the agent can return to the actual Context-surface and mark the imagined route as non-realized. In pathological or unstable states, imagined belief-paths can become over-bound to reality-selection and compete with actual Perception for control of Belief-intention.

Imagination is therefore a necessary extension of agency. An agent that could only process immediately executable beliefs would be trapped in the present surface. It could not plan, rehearse, regret, hope, fear, invent, empathize, or repair. Imagination allows the agent to use the same PABEN loops ahead of action, beside action, and after action.

## Presence and Possibility: Inside-Out Rather Than Outside-In
The model treats presence as inside-out. The agent does not begin with an external picture that is then interpreted. It begins with active continuity: Needs press, Experience carries lived consequences, Beliefs open possible routes, Action prepares skills, and Perception confirms or disturbs the surface. What appears as “the world present before me” is the current field of contexts that can carry belief and action.

Perception is therefore not the origin of presence. Perception confirms, updates, and corrects presence. A context can remain present when the agent looks away because the context continues to be carried by belief, memory, expectation, and action-readiness. A cup behind the body, a person in the next room, a meeting later today, a social obligation, a threat, or a desired object can remain present because it remains part of the agent’s active belief-structure.

The Context-surface is the currently exposed edge of this deeper continuity. It is where perception meets the belief-field. It does not contain everything the agent knows. It contains the contexts currently available, disruptive, or relevant for process-control. Behind it lies the Belief-horizon: the wider route-structure the agent can still hold as possible.

Possibility is also inside-out. The world does not simply present all possible actions to the agent. Possibilities are opened when recognized contexts meet learned skills, prior episodes, active Needs, and belief-selection. A chair is not present as a neutral object first and an affordance later. It is recognized as a context that can support sitting, moving, blocking, standing on, offering, throwing, repairing, or ignoring, depending on the belief-field and Need-state.

This shifts the ordinary outside-in description. The agent is not a spectator receiving a world and then deciding what it means. The agent is a continuity-seeking system whose learned belief-routes make parts of the world show up as usable, dangerous, attractive, irrelevant, blocked, sacred, social, or unknown. Perception constrains the agent, but it does not alone define the agent’s world. The agent’s world is the field of possible continuation.

## Belief and Felt Need as the Experience of Continuity
The lived experience of the agent is not raw perception alone. It is the coupling of belief and felt Need. A belief gives direction: from here, this can be done, and this route may carry. Felt Need gives importance: this matters, this presses, this relieves, this threatens, this fulfills. Together they form the experience of being a continuing agent.

A belief without felt Need would be empty possibility. It could describe a route, but nothing would make the route matter. A Need without belief would be pressure without path. It would be felt demand without actionable continuation. Experience becomes agentic when Need-pressure and belief-route meet: the agent feels that something matters and that something can or must be done.

This is why the model does not place emotion outside cognition. Emotion is the felt deformation of expectancy. When a belief-path opens, closes, strengthens, breaks, or changes direction under Need relevance, the agent feels the change. The feeling is not decoration. It is the lived signal that continuity has changed.

The self is therefore not added after the processes. The self is the ongoing continuity of belief under felt need. The agent experiences itself as the one who must continue, because Needs are felt as its own pressures and beliefs are lived as its own possible routes. This does not require a separate inner observer. It requires that belief-paths and Need-pressure are bound in one continuity.

This also helps explain why social dependency can shape selfhood. In early life, many belief-routes are carried by others. Caregivers regulate Needs, provide contexts, select actions, and maintain routes to relief before the infant can do so alone. The infant’s continuity is real, but it is externally supported. As shared routes are internalized, the agent becomes able to carry more of its own belief-continuity. Private agency emerges from stabilized shared continuity.

## Memory as Traversal Through Belief-Loops
Memory is not the retrieval of a stored picture. In the PABEN model, memory is traversal through episode-linked belief-loops. An episode stores a lived transition: from a context, through a lived belief, with a felt need-consequence, to a resulting context. Remembering is the reactivation and traversal of such structures.

When the agent remembers, it does not simply display the past. It re-enters a route-field. A remembered context can open the beliefs that were live there. A remembered belief can reopen the felt need-consequence that gave the episode its meaning. A remembered feeling can retrieve the contexts and beliefs through which it was lived. Memory therefore runs through loops:

$$C→E→B→S/C→E$$

The remembered event becomes present because the agent traverses the structure that once carried continuity.

This helps explain why memory is selective. The agent does not remember everything equally. It remembers what is linked to continuity: pain, relief, danger, success, shame, pride, loss, attachment, unfinished pressure, unresolved route-breaks, or repeated stable paths. Episode structures with strong dX or unresolved pressure remain easier to traverse because they still matter for current or future continuation.

Memory can also be constructive without being arbitrary. When the agent remembers, it can fill gaps from related belief-structures, context siblings, skill patterns, and emotional consequences. This is not merely error. It is route-reconstruction. The agent rebuilds the past as a traversable structure from the materials that still connect.

Rumination is repeated traversal of unresolved belief-loops. The agent returns again and again to a route that did not close, a Need that was not regulated, a social judgement that was not repaired, a loss that did not find integration, or a threat that still lacks avoidance. Rumination persists because the loop still demands continuity. It is not pointless repetition from the system’s perspective. It is failed or incomplete stabilization.

Remembering can also heal. If the agent traverses an old episode through a new belief, a new context, a new social recognition, or a new felt consequence, the episode can be re-bound. The past is not erased, but its route-position changes. A memory that previously ended in helplessness can become a memory that leads to understanding, repair, grief, forgiveness, or acceptance. This is possible because memory is traversal through belief-loops, not playback of fixed images.

## Consciousness as the Duty and Freedom of Continuation
The same conscious machinery supports duty and freedom. Under Need-pressure, consciousness is obligated. It must find or preserve a route. Under surplus, consciousness becomes free to play, imagine, rehearse, explore, and remember without immediate external realization. In both cases, consciousness is the processual occupation of PABEN structures for the sake of continuity.

This assigns consciousness a more precise role. It is the agent’s active mode of continuity-stabilization where automatic structure is insufficient. When the route is already learned and stable, consciousness can withdraw. When the route is unstable, consciousness returns. It returns as action, observation, reflection, feeling, surprise, imagination, or memory, depending on where the continuity problem lies.

The model therefore does not need to separate consciousness from agency. Consciousness is what agency becomes when continuation must be actively held. It is not a mirror of the world. It is not a container of representations. It is not an inner spectator. It is the agent occupying the part of its own structure where the next viable continuation must be secured.

This helps explain why consciousness is best treated as exclusive in this model. A conscious process is a commitment. The agent cannot fully commit to executing, varying, reflecting, feeling, and inspecting at the same time. Each mode binds the system differently. Each mode answers a different continuity problem. Consciousness is unified because only one such commitment can dominate at a time.

The result is a model in which attention, process-control, imagination, presence, feeling, memory, and consciousness all arise from the same structural demand. The agent must continue. When it must continue under pressure, it feels obligation. When it can continue with surplus, it plays. When the world changes, it is surprised. When the body changes, it feels. When the route breaks, it reflects. When routes are stable enough to be replayed without full consequence, it imagines and remembers. Consciousness is the live occupation of these continuity-preserving processes.
