# 4. Core Concepts and Definitions

This section defines the central concepts used in the PABEN model. The terms are used in a technical sense. The purpose is to stabilize the model vocabulary before the formal description is introduced.
###	Agent
An agent is a system that must continue from its present situation toward the regulation or fulfillment of its needs.
Agency requires a situated context, available skills, prior experience, active needs, and an evaluative mechanism that selects viable continuations.
###	Continuation
Continuation is the agent’s movement from its current context into a next viable state, action, belief, or need-regulating path.
Continuation is the preservation of a possible path from where the agent is, through what it can do, toward what matters.
###	PABEN
PABEN is the five-domain architecture of the model:
Perception, Action, Beliefs, Experience, and Needs.
Perception recognizes contexts. Action releases skills. Beliefs bind contexts and skills. Experience releases possible beliefs from learned transitions. Needs determine why continuation matters.
###	Perception / P
Perception is the domain of contexts.
It recognizes features, objects, relations, causes, and situations. Perception identifies the context the agent is in and opens possible actions from that context.
Perception is distinct from felt need-states. The feeling of cold belongs to Needs. An open window, a cold surface, or a draft belongs to Perception.
###	Context / C
A context is a recognized situation, structure, object, relation, or cause that matters for action.
Contexts are possibility-fields. They constrain, release, and stabilize possible skills and beliefs. They are hierarchical in nature, where the lowest level is activated by the sensory features of the world. 
A context can be concrete, such as a cup on a table, or abstract, such as being in a meeting, being responsible for a task, or being in a social role.
###	Context-actual
Context-actual is the currently active context-field in which the agent is situated.
It is the carrying context structure from which Experience can release possible beliefs and Action can release selected skills. Context-actual can include directly perceived elements and persistent context elements that remain active without direct sampling. The highest level of recognition is defined as the Context-surface.
###	Action / A
Action is the domain of skills.
It contains the executable patterns by which the agent can do something. Action includes bodily action, attention-shifting, speaking, social behavior, manipulation, searching, waiting, practicing, and other structured capacities the agent can release.
###	Skill / S
A skill is an executable action-pattern.
A skill is what the agent can do when the relevant context is active. Skills become usable through their relation to contexts and beliefs. The lowest level skill is the motor activation in the world.
###	Belief / B
A belief is an action-belief.
It is a functional expectation that a skill can be applied in a context. In its simplest form, a belief says: while in this context, this skill can be used.
A belief binds Perception and Action together. It is the agent’s usable claim about how continuation can proceed from a recognized situation.
###	Belief-field
A Belief-field is the current set of action-beliefs that can be selected now.
The Belief-field is released from Experience based on context-actual, the active need-state, and prior learned transitions. It contains the agent’s available ways forward.
Some beliefs are strong and ready for execution. Others are weak, uncertain, blocked, risky, latent, or only partly available. The selected belief becomes attended, intended, and possibly released into action.
###	Belief-horizon
The Belief-horizon is the strategic layer of available continuation.
It is the level at which the agent can hold an abstract belief-route stable as a viable expectation toward future valence. It is defined by the highest level of abstraction at which the agent can keep a route stable.

The Belief-field contains what can be selected now. The Belief-horizon contains the experienced routes the agent can hold as possible ways forward.
A high-level route can be stable even when its lower-level contexts, skills, and sub-beliefs still require local stabilization during execution.

A short Belief-horizon limits the agent to immediate and concrete continuations. A long Belief-horizon allows the agent to maintain indirect, delayed, and abstract paths toward fulfillment.
### Experience / E
Experience is the domain of learned transitions.
Experience records what happened when beliefs were used. It binds the starting context, the applied belief, the resulting context or need-state, and the valence consequence of the transition.
Experience releases possible beliefs into the current Belief-field and provides the route-structure held in the Belief-horizon.
### Episode
An episode is an instance of transition: a context, a belief used, a need felt, and a transition.
Episodes are the material from which Experience is built. Repeated episodes strengthen belief-connections, context-skill relations, and expected continuations.
### Needs / N
Needs are the domain of pressure, valence, and fulfillment.
Needs make continuation matter. They create pressure for the agent to find, preserve, restore, transform, or avoid a path.
### Valence
Valence is the felt positive or negative significance of a state, path, or outcome relative to Needs.
Valence marks whether a path opens, preserves, restores, blocks, threatens, or loses access to fulfillment.
### Fulfillment
Fulfillment is the regulation, satisfaction, relief, completion, or stabilization of a Need.
Fulfillment can be complete satisfaction, partial relief, restored access, renewed possibility, or sufficient stabilization for continuation.
### Expectancy / X
Expectancy, abbreviated X, is the viability of a possible path.
X describes whether a belief-path can hold as a continuation from the present context toward valence or fulfillment.
A belief has high X when the required context is stable, the skill can be executed, the path can continue, and the path preserves access to what matters.
A belief has low X when the required context, skill, continuation, or value is unstable, blocked, uncertain, or lost.
### Trustability / T
Trustability is the stability of the required context.
T concerns whether the context needed for a belief holds sufficiently for the belief to be used.
### Executability / Q
Executability is the release- and execution-stability of the required skill.
Q concerns whether the agent can release and carry out the skill needed for a belief.
Q does not mean general quality or value. It means whether the skill can be executed.
### Reachability / R
Reachability is the continuation-strength of the current path.
R concerns whether there are viable next steps from the present context through Experience. It describes whether the continuation-field remains open, connected, and navigable.
### Value / V
Value is the degree to which a path preserves, restores, approaches, avoids, or transforms access to valence and fulfillment.
V is why the path matters. T, Q, and R describe whether the path can hold, execute, and continue. V describes why the path is worth holding, executing, and continuing.
### Path
A path is a possible continuation from the present context through belief, skill, experience, and need-regulation.
A path is a viability structure: a way for the agent to keep moving toward what matters.
### Path viability
Path viability is the degree to which a path can hold, be executed, continue, and matter.
Path viability is evaluated through Trustability, Executability, Reachability, and Value, and summarized by expectancy X.
### Emotion
Emotion is the felt direction and amplitude of a change in expectancy.
When X changes, the agent feels that a path has become more or less possible, promising, dangerous, stable, blocked, realized, or lost.
Emotion guides attention, learning, and process switching.
### Attention
Attention is the pointer to one belief.
Attention points to the current problem-belief: the belief whose unresolved expectancy, instability, promise, threat, or consequence matters most.
Attention does not solve the problem. It marks which belief the system must work on.
### Consciousness
Consciousness is the active process over the attended belief.
Consciousness is the necessary activity carried out over the problem-belief in the necessary domain. Depending on what the belief requires, consciousness can occupy Perception, Action, Belief, Experience, or Need-related stabilization.
Where attention points, consciousness works.
### Recognize–Execute
Recognize–Execute is the fluent process mode.
A context is recognized, Experience releases a Belief-field, one belief becomes dominant, and a skill is released.
The agent continues without reconstructing each step because Perception, Belief, and Action are already coordinated.
### Try–Observe
Try–Observe is the exploratory learning mode.
The agent holds a context in attention and varies skills to observe what changes, what remains stable, what the context can bear, and which variants belong together.
Try–Observe drives play, practice, curiosity, and local context-skill learning.
### Reflection
Reflection is the process mode that searches for hooks and reconstructs possible continuation when the current Belief-field lacks a sufficiently viable path.
Reflection works on the Belief-horizon. It connects the present situation to a known or possible valence-bearing route.
Reflection does not create meaning from nothing. It searches for a route back into meaningful continuation.
### Hook
A hook is a connection from a current unstable, unresolved, or new situation into a known or possible valence-bearing Belief-field or route.
A new belief becomes meaningful when it hooks into a known field of fulfillment, relief, avoidance, repair, or continuation.
Valence-backward learning
Valence-backward learning is the principle that the model learns meaning from valence backwards.
The agent discovers places where pain, relief, appetite, threat, satisfaction, or fulfillment occur, and then builds context-skill-belief structure backward from those valenced outcomes.
### Learning
Learning is the establishment or strengthening of structure through attention, priming, co-activation, simultaneity, emotion, and consequence.
Learning is constructive rather than editorial. The agent builds new connections, hooks, and pathways. The model does not rely on a central mechanism that rewrites the whole structure.
### Priming
Priming is the activation of related contexts, skills, beliefs, experiences, or needs so they become available for selection or learning.
Priming prepares possible continuations before one of them is selected or released.
### Co-activation
Co-activation is the simultaneous activation of elements across domains, such as a context, a skill, a need-state, an emotion, and an outcome.
Co-activation establishes and strengthens relations in the model.
### Surprise
Surprise occurs when something is recognized outside the active context or expected continuation-field.
Surprise is the appearance of something that does not belong to the currently carrying context or Belief-field and therefore requires evaluation.
### Zip / Context-Skill Zip
The zip is the coordinated unfolding of context and skill during action.
As the agent acts, contexts release skills and skills lead into new contexts. In learned action, this unfolds fluently without conscious reconstruction at each step.
### Social context
A social context is a context in which beliefs, actions, and outcomes are evaluated in relation to other agents and shared provider-contexts.
Social contexts contain fields of permission, recognition, responsibility, care, repair, status, and belonging.
### Social emotion
A social emotion is an emotion arising from changes in expectancy within a shared or socially judged context.
Social emotions concern how the agent’s or another agent’s beliefs and actions align or misalign with the shared context and its expected value.
