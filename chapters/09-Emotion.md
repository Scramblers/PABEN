# Emotion, Self-Emotion, and Social Emotion

The PABEN model treats emotion as the felt deformation of expectancy. Emotion is not added after cognition. Emotion is the control signal that appears when the viability of a belief-path changes. When the agent’s ability to continue through a belief is improved, opened, threatened, blocked, confirmed, lost, or socially judged, this change is felt as emotion.

Expectancy, X, expresses the viability of a belief-path through Trustability, Executability, Reachability, and Value:

$$X=T⋅Q⋅R⋅V$$

Emotion arises when this viability changes:

$$dX=X_{after}-X_{before}$$

A positive deformation means that the belief-path has become more viable. A negative deformation means that it has become less viable. The amplitude of the deformation determines the force of the emotion. A small dX may be registered as a slight preference, tension, curiosity, irritation, or relief. A large dX can seize attention, reorganize process-control, narrow Need-consideration, and force immediate action or Reflection.

Emotion is therefore not a representation of an object. It is the agent’s felt update on its own continuity. The object, context, person, or event matters because it changes the viability of a belief-path. The same object can produce different emotions depending on which belief it opens, blocks, confirms, threatens, or completes. The emotion belongs to the deformation of the path, not to the object alone.

## Belief-States and Emotional Position
The emotional quality of dX depends on where the deformation occurs in the belief-process. A belief can be disturbed at the moment of recognition, opened as a promise, carried into realization, or closed through failure. These are not merely different intensities of the same feeling. They are different process-positions.

Surprise belongs to recognition. It appears when the Context-surface changes in a belief-relevant way. A new context can open an unexpected route, threaten an existing route, block an intended route, or reveal that the current route was misrecognized. Surprise is therefore emotional recognition at the Context-surface. It is not raw novelty. It is the sudden deformation of the Belief-field caused by what the surface now exposes.

Potential emotion belongs to the open belief. When a belief becomes intended, the agent begins to live a promise. The promise says: if this belief can be carried, the agent may reach, avoid, repair, regulate, or fulfill something that matters. The belief is not yet closed. Its outcome is not yet secured. This open state produces potential emotion. If the promise is appetitive, it is felt as desire. If the promise concerns threat, loss, damage, or aversive continuation, it is felt as fear.

Realized emotion belongs to successful closure. When the lived belief raises X, the agent feels joy. Joy is not a reward added after success. It is the felt increase in viability when the path has carried. The agent has passed through uncertainty, effort, risk, or lack into a state where continuation is more open, stable, reachable, or fulfilled. When the lived belief lowers X through actual loss, damage, pain, or irreversible negative consequence, the agent feels sorrow. Sorrow is the felt deformation of a path that has become less viable because something valuable can no longer be carried as before.

Failed emotion belongs to non-realization. A positive promise can fail to become what it promised. This produces disappointment. A negative expectation can fail to occur. This produces relief. Relief is therefore not simply positive surprise. It is the failed realization of an expected negative continuation. Disappointment and relief are both closure-states: the agent learns that an expected path did not become actual.

|---|---|---|
| Surprise | Appetitive | Aversive |
| Potential | Desire | Fear |

## Promise: The Open Belief
A promise is the state of a belief when it has become intended but has not yet been closed as lived. The agent has committed to the belief as a route, but the route has not yet proved whether it can carry. This is the basic emotional structure of expectation.

The promise is not verbal. It is structural. A belief promises that from this context, through this skill, a route can be lived toward a context where something can be handled. In compact form, the belief binds a required context and a prepared skill. When it is lived, it becomes an episode. Until it is closed, it is a promise.

The promise creates exposure. While a belief is open, the agent is vulnerable to what happens to the route. If Trustability falls, the context no longer carries. If Executability falls, the skill no longer holds. If Reachability falls, the route no longer reaches the valence-bearing field. If Value falls, the route no longer matters or no longer relieves the active pressure. Each deformation changes the felt state of the agent.

This explains why emotion is inseparable from action. To intend a belief is to expose oneself to the possibility that the world carries or fails to carry it. The agent does not merely think about the promise. It lives it. Emotion is the felt monitoring of whether the promise remains viable.

A promise can also remain unresolved. If the belief neither succeeds nor fails cleanly, its emotional charge can persist. The open belief remains unfinished in Experience. The agent may return to it through rumination, longing, fear, resentment, guilt, hope, or repeated Reflection. Unresolved emotion is therefore not a mysterious residue. It is an unclosed promise whose $X$ remains unstable.

## Self-Emotion: The Agent Judging Its Own Belief-Path
Self-emotion arises when the deformation of X concerns the agent’s own belief-path. The agent feels that its own route has opened, carried, failed, threatened, collapsed, or become unreachable. This includes the basic emotions, but it also includes more structured self-emotions such as pride, shame, guilt, disappointment, relief, longing, fear, sorrow, and joy when they concern the agent’s own ability to continue.

The self is not a detached observer of these emotions. The self is the continuity of belief under felt Need. The agent experiences itself as the one who must continue because the pressure is felt from within and the belief is lived as its own route. A self-emotion is therefore an emotion in which the agent’s own continuity is at stake.

Pride, for example, is not only successful action. It is successful action under a belief that can be owned. The agent has lived a belief, carried a skill, reached a context, and strengthened its own expectancy. In social contexts, pride also requires recognition: the shared context confirms that the belief was valid and that the agent’s contribution belongs in the group-field.

Shame is not merely negative feeling about the self. It is the collapse of a socially or internally important belief-route in which the agent itself becomes the failed carrier. The agent does not only feel that something bad happened. It feels that its own belief-use, action, exposure, or visibility has damaged its place in a valued context. Shame therefore threatens continuity through the agent’s relation to a context it still depends on.

Guilt concerns damage done by the agent’s belief-use. The agent has lived or allowed a belief that lowered the viability of another agent, a shared context, or a valued route. Guilt therefore demands repair. It is not closed by denial. It is closed by re-establishing the route that was damaged, or by making amends in a way that restores the shared belief-field.

These emotions show that self-emotion is already relational. The agent’s own belief-path is never purely private. It is learned through contexts, episodes, Needs, skills, and other agents. The self is the local carrier of continuity, but its routes are embedded in shared and inherited structures.

## Social Emotion: Shared Beliefs and Emotional Contracts
Social emotion arises when the deformation of X occurs inside shared belief-dependencies. A shared belief is a route that more than one agent depends on, recognizes, carries, judges, or is expected to preserve. Social emotion begins when the viability of my path depends on how another agent carries, confirms, blocks, damages, repairs, or recognizes a belief.

A shared belief creates an emotional contract. This contract does not need to be explicit. It is built into dependency. If I depend on you to carry a context, your action can raise or lower my X. If you depend on me to carry a context, my action can raise or lower yours. If a group depends on a shared belief, each participant becomes emotionally exposed to how the others use it.

This is why social emotions are not reducible to private feelings. Anger, gratitude, shame, pride, envy, jealousy, guilt, humiliation, compassion, respect, betrayal, and loneliness all involve belief-dependencies that cross agents. They concern who carried the shared context, who damaged it, who received access, who was denied access, who gave support, who failed to respond, who was recognized, and who was excluded.

The social layer therefore interprets dX through four belief-dimensions: action/skill contribution, context preservation or disturbance, access and recognition, and fulfillment through attachment or care. These dimensions determine what kind of social emotion appears. If another agent supports a route I depend on, gratitude becomes available. If I support another agent’s route, duty or care becomes available. If another agent damages a shared context, indignation or anger becomes available. If I damage it, guilt or shame becomes available. If I am granted access to a valued group-context, pride and recognition become available. If I am denied access, humiliation, longing, jealousy, or exclusion-pressure can arise.

A social emotion is therefore a control signal for shared-context stability. It tells the agent that a shared belief-route has been opened, confirmed, damaged, blocked, misused, repaired, or lost. The emotion does not only inform the individual. It pressures the social field to respond.

## Recognition, Gratitude, Love, Forgiveness, and Social Closure
Because social emotions arise from shared belief-tensions, they contain implicit pressure toward social closure. The emotion is not complete until the shared route has either been held, confirmed, repaired, reorganized, abandoned, or protected.

Recognition closes a positive contribution arc. When an agent uses a belief in a way that supports a valued shared context, recognition confirms that the contribution belongs. Recognition stabilizes the agent’s access to the group-context and allows pride to close. Without recognition, the positive action remains socially unclosed. The agent may experience longing, frustration, humiliation, or withdrawal, because the route was lived but not socially confirmed.

Gratitude closes a support arc. When one agent carries a route that another agent depends on, the receiver is expected to register the contribution. Gratitude is not politeness added to support. It is the social confirmation that the giver’s belief-use had value. Without gratitude, the giver’s contribution is socially devalued. This can create resentment, sorrow, withdrawal, or anger, because the giver’s action carried the shared context without being registered as carrying it.

Love closes a continuity arc. Love appears where another agent, relation, group, or shared context is felt as keeping access to valence open. The loved context is not merely pleasant. It is a carrier of future viability: a place where the agent’s Needs, beliefs, vulnerabilities, and possible continuations can still be held. Love therefore stabilizes attachment to contexts that preserve access to fulfillment, relief, recognition, care, and shared presence. To love is to experience another as a condition under which continuation remains deeply open.

Love also creates obligation. If another agent carries my access to valence, then their presence, safety, recognition, and continuity become part of my own X. Their loss, withdrawal, suffering, or rejection lowers my route-viability. Their joy, safety, recognition, and continued presence can raise it. Love therefore expands the self’s continuity into a shared field. The other is not external to the path. The other becomes part of the path’s ability to remain open.

Forgiveness closes a damage arc. When an agent damages a valued shared belief-field, guilt, shame, anger, or indignation appear because the route has been lowered. Forgiveness is not the mere cancellation of anger. It is the social process by which the damaged belief-field is repaired enough for the offender to be reintegrated. Amends must be made: some form of amends, humility, acknowledgement, or repair that shows the offender now carries the damaged context differently. Without repair, forgiveness cannot stabilize the field. If forgiveness is given without restored trust, the shared context remains structurally unsafe.

Exclusion closes a protection arc. If an agent repeatedly damages the internal belief-field and does not repair the damage, the group may protect itself by excluding the agent. Exclusion is not only punishment. It is a way of preserving the internal Trustability of a shared context. The group removes a source of high variance so that the remaining belief-field can continue. From the excluded agent’s side, this appears as loss of access, humiliation, loneliness, or resentment. From the group’s side, it can appear as relief, anger, moral certainty, or defensive pride.

These social closure processes show that emotions are not only signals. They are action-tendencies toward re-stabilization. Recognition confirms contribution. Gratitude registers support. Love holds access to shared valence open. Forgiveness repairs damage. Exclusion protects the internal belief-field when repair fails. Together, they describe how social systems close emotional tension by confirming, registering, holding, repairing, or protecting the routes on which agents depend.

## Emotional Contracts and the Logic of Social Obligation
Social life is held together by implicit belief-contracts. These contracts specify what must be carried for the shared context to remain usable. They exist wherever agents depend on each other’s beliefs.

Care implies gratitude. If one agent repeatedly supports another’s route, the receiver is expected to register and respect the contribution. Gratitude confirms that the support was not invisible. Without gratitude, care becomes socially unstable.

Compassion implies respect. If one agent responds to another’s suffering, the receiver is expected to remain a recognized agent rather than become an object of pity. Respect prevents compassion from becoming domination or humiliation.

Damage implies repair. If one agent lowers the viability of another’s route or of a shared context, the damaging agent is expected to acknowledge, repair, or make amends. Without repair, guilt and shame cannot close, and anger or exclusion-pressure remains active.

Contribution implies recognition. If one agent successfully carries a belief that benefits the shared context, the group is expected to confirm the contribution. Without recognition, pride cannot close and the agent’s access to the shared context remains unstable.

These contracts are not moral rules imposed from outside the model. They follow from the mechanics of shared X. If my path depends on your belief-use, your action changes my expectancy. If your path depends on mine, my action changes yours. Social emotion is the felt control layer that keeps these mutual dependencies from dissolving into unpredictability.

## Facial Expression as Communication of Internal State
Facial expression can be understood as social communication of the agent’s internal dX state. An expression does not merely display a private feeling. It exposes the status of the agent’s belief-path to others. It says, in bodily form, that a route has opened, failed, threatened, carried, collapsed, or requires response.

Fear signals that the agent’s route is threatened and that aversive continuation is possible. Anger signals that a context or belief-dependency has been damaged or blocked and that correction is required. Joy signals that a route has carried and that continuation has opened. Sorrow signals that a valued route has collapsed or become unavailable. Shame signals that the agent’s own position in a valued context has been lowered. Pride signals that the agent’s contribution or route has been confirmed.

In social contexts, expression is part of the regulatory machinery. A face can request help, warn others, demand repair, invite recognition, signal submission, offer reconciliation, show gratitude, display exclusion-pressure, or mark that a shared belief-field is unsafe. The expression changes the Belief-field of other agents. It is therefore not only an output of emotion. It is an action within the shared context.

This explains why emotion becomes visible. Social agents need access to each other’s internal route-status because their paths are interdependent. If another agent is afraid, angry, ashamed, proud, grateful, or hurt, that state matters for the shared belief-field. Facial expression externalizes enough of the internal dX state for others to adjust their own beliefs, actions, and expectations.

Expression is also costly and therefore socially meaningful. To show shame, gratitude, grief, anger, pride, or fear is to expose one’s relation to the shared context. The face makes the internal promise-status visible: “I can carry this,” “I cannot carry this,” “you damaged this,” “I damaged this,” “I need repair,” “I recognize your contribution,” “I am still with you,” or “I am no longer safe here.” Facial expression is the social surface of belief-continuity.

## Emotion, Attention, and Process-Switching
Emotion controls attention because dX indicates where continuity is changing. A strong negative deformation pulls attention toward stabilization. A strong positive deformation can pull attention toward opportunity, desire, attachment, play, or completion. Emotion is therefore the bridge between evaluation and process-control.

When surprise occurs at the Context-surface, attention moves outward. The agent must inspect what the world now exposes. When fear or desire appears in an open promise, attention stays with the intended belief or the route it opens. When joy or sorrow appears through realization, the agent registers closure and updates Experience. When disappointment or relief appears through failed realization, the agent updates the promise-structure and adjusts future expectancy.

Social emotion adds another layer of process-switching. Anger can move the agent toward correction, boundary-setting, or exclusion. Guilt can move the agent toward repair. Shame can move the agent toward hiding, apology, humility, or re-entry through forgiveness. Gratitude can move the agent toward acknowledgement and reciprocity. Pride can move the agent toward further responsibility in the shared context. Loneliness can move the agent toward reconnection. Compassion can move the agent toward care.

The model does not need to assign separate modules to these behaviors. The emotion changes X, attention follows the deformation, and process-control selects the required route: Recognise–Execute if the route is available, Try–Observe if the relation must be stabilized, Reflection if a new hook is needed, Need-feeling if pressure itself must be occupied, or Perceptual inspection if the Context-surface has changed.

## Emotional Learning and Episode Update
Emotion also marks which episodes matter for learning. A lived transition with little dX may be stored weakly. A transition with strong dX changes future expectancy. The agent learns from what changed viability.

Positive closure strengthens the episode-field. If a belief carried and raised X, future Belief-fields will make that route more available. Negative closure weakens or breaks the route. If a belief lowered X, produced loss, damaged a context, or failed to reach relief, future expectancy falls. Failed expectations update the promise-structure: the agent learns that a predicted positive or negative continuation did not become actual.

Social learning follows the same logic. A shared belief that produced recognition, gratitude, forgiveness, or repair becomes more stable. A shared belief that produced shame, anger, humiliation, betrayal, or exclusion becomes socially marked. The agent learns not only what works in the physical world, but which belief-uses are allowed, recognized, dangerous, humiliating, respected, or forbidden in shared contexts.

Emotional learning therefore gives the model its history. Contexts do not merely become more familiar. They become valence-bearing, safe, unsafe, shameful, proud, desired, feared, trusted, sacred, excluded, or socially charged. Skills do not merely become more efficient. They become allowed, forbidden, admired, embarrassing, powerful, dangerous, caring, or aggressive. Beliefs do not merely become probable. They become routes the agent can or cannot live.
